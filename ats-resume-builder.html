Most large companies use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) to filter applications before a human ever reads them. A document with the wrong formatting — tables, columns, graphics, or unusual fonts — can be misread or rejected automatically, no matter how qualified you are. Research suggests up to 75% of applications are filtered out by ATS software before reaching a recruiter.

You still get a polished, professional look in standard mode for direct human review — and you can switch to ATS Mode with one click whenever you are submitting through an online application portal. The same data, two export formats, no rebuilding required.

Understanding how ATS software actually works is the key to beating it. These systems extract text from your uploaded file, parse it into structured fields (name, email, work history, education, skills), and match those fields against the job description. If the parser cannot read a section — because it is in a table, an image, or a non-standard font — that information simply disappears from your application.

The most common parsing failures come from multi-column layouts, text inside text boxes, graphics and icons used as bullets, headers and footers containing critical information, and non-standard section headings. ATS Mode in this builder eliminates all of these risks by using a strict single-column layout with conventional headings that every major parser recognises.

Beyond formatting, ATS systems also match keywords from your document against the job description. The builder helps here too — the Skills section encourages you to list terms that mirror the job posting's language, and the AI assist can suggest relevant skills based on the job title you enter. Just remember that keyword stuffing (repeating terms unnaturally) is detected and penalised — natural, contextual usage is what works.

Once you have built your document in ATS Mode, export as PDF. Despite a persistent myth that ATS systems prefer DOCX files, modern platforms parse both formats equally well. PDF has the advantage of preserving your layout exactly as you designed it, which is why most recruiters now expect PDFs from applicants at every level.

What to include

Standard section headings

Use "Work Experience", "Education", "Skills", "Certifications" — not "My Journey", "Academic Background", or "Toolkit". ATS parsers look for conventional headings to categorise your information. Creative headings get ignored.

A clean, single-column layout

Avoid sidebars, two-column structures, and text boxes. Information should flow top-to-bottom in a single column. If you need a sidebar for contact details, place them at the top instead — that is where parsers expect to find them.

Standard fonts in 10-12pt

Arial, Calibri, Helvetica, Georgia, or Times New Roman. Avoid custom fonts that may not be embedded in the PDF. Non-standard fonts cause parsers to substitute characters, which can corrupt names and technical terms.

Dates in a consistent format

Pick one format ("Mar 2021 – Present" or "03/2021 – Present") and use it for every role. Inconsistent date formats confuse parsers and can cause your work history to be categorised incorrectly.

Job titles and employers clearly labelled

Format each role as "Job Title – Employer" or use separate lines. Avoid embedding the employer name inside a paragraph. Parsers look for clear title/employer pairs to populate their structured fields.

Skills as plain text, not graphics

List skills as comma-separated text or simple bullet points. Avoid progress bars, star ratings, or coloured chips. These visual elements do not parse and your skills will not be recognised.

Tips for a stronger resume

Mirror the job description's exact terminology

If the posting asks for "stakeholder management", use that phrase rather than "client relations". ATS systems match on exact terms. Just ensure the usage is natural — keyword stuffing is detected and rejected.

Save with a clean, professional filename

"Jane_Doe_Resume.pdf" is ideal. Avoid spaces, special characters, and version numbers in the filename. Some ATS platforms struggle with unusual filenames and may fail to attach your document to the application.

Avoid headers and footers for critical information

Some ATS parsers skip headers and footers entirely. Keep your contact details in the main body of the document, not in a repeating header. Page numbers in the footer are fine — important information is not.

Spell out acronyms on first use

"Search Engine Optimisation (SEO)" on first mention, then "SEO" thereafter. This ensures the parser picks up both the full term and the acronym, maximising your keyword match score.

Use standard bullet characters

Round bullets (•) or hyphens (-) parse reliably. Avoid custom bullet icons (stars, checkmarks, arrows) which can confuse the parser or be stripped entirely.

Test your PDF by copying text from it

Open your exported PDF, select all text, and paste it into a plain text editor. If the text reads in a logical order with no jumbled sections, the ATS parser will likely read it correctly too. If the text is garbled, fix the formatting and re-export.

Apply through the company's website when possible

Direct applications on the company's careers page usually go straight into their ATS. Email applications may be parsed separately or manually entered, which can introduce errors. When a posting offers both options, choose the website.

Common mistakes to avoid

Using multi-column layouts

Two-column templates look elegant but break ATS parsing. The parser reads left-to-right across the full page width, mixing content from both columns into nonsense. Stick to a single column for any application going through an online portal.

Embedding text inside images

If your name and contact details are part of a header image, the parser cannot read them. Your application will arrive with no name attached. Use plain text for all critical information.

Using tables for layout

Tables confuse ATS parsers — they may treat each cell as a separate entry or skip the table entirely. Use plain paragraphs and bullet points instead. Save tables for actual tabular data (rarely needed on a CV).

Choosing unconventional section headings

"My Professional Journey" instead of "Work Experience" will not be recognised by the parser. Your work history may end up uncategorised or dumped into a generic "Additional Information" bucket. Use the standard headings every recruiter and parser expects.

Stuffing keywords invisibly

White text on a white background, tiny font sizes, or repeated keywords in metadata are all detected by modern ATS systems. Your application will be flagged as spam and rejected. Write naturally for human readers — the keywords will appear organically.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an ATS and why does it matter?

An Applicant Tracking System is software employers use to scan, parse, and filter applications before a recruiter sees them. Up to 75% of submissions are filtered out automatically. Poor formatting can cause a qualified application to be rejected before any human reviews it.

How does ATS Mode help?

ATS Mode removes decorative styling and uses a clean, single-column layout with standard section headings and fonts. This is the structure that ATS parsers read most reliably, maximising the chance your information is extracted correctly.

Is the ATS resume builder free?

Yes, it is completely free to build and download your ATS-optimised document. There is no signup, no watermark, and no premium tier. You can produce as many versions as you need at no cost.

Can I still download a styled version for direct applications?

Yes, you can toggle between a styled version and ATS Mode at any time before exporting. Use the styled version for direct emails to recruiters, networking events, and in-person interviews.

Does ATS Mode remove my profile photo?

Yes, photos are automatically hidden in ATS Mode since most ATS platforms cannot process images and they can corrupt the parsing of nearby text. Switch back to styled mode if you are sending the document directly to a recruiter in a market where photos are expected.

Should I send PDF or DOCX to an ATS?

Modern ATS platforms parse both PDF and DOCX reliably. PDF is generally safer because it preserves your formatting exactly and cannot be accidentally edited. Send DOCX only if the employer explicitly requests an editable file.

How do I know if my document will parse correctly?

After exporting, open the PDF, select all text, and paste it into a plain text editor. If the text reads in a logical order with no jumbled sections, the parser will likely read it correctly. If it is garbled, simplify the layout and re-export.

Can I include graphics and charts in an ATS-friendly document?

No. Graphics, icons, charts, and progress bars cannot be parsed by ATS software and may even break the parsing of nearby text. Save the visual elements for your portfolio or LinkedIn profile — keep your application document as plain text.
