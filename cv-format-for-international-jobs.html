Applying internationally means your CV needs to adapt — different countries have different expectations around photos, personal details, length, and structure. Rather than building several CVs from scratch, you need a flexible format you can adjust per application. This builder is designed for exactly that workflow.

This builder lets you toggle exactly the sections and details that matter: add or remove a profile photo, include or hide your address, and reorder sections to match the expectations of the country you are applying to. The same underlying data can produce a US-style one-page resume, a UK-style two-page CV, a UAE-style three-page CV with photo, or a German-style academic CV — all with a few toggle changes.

Use one tool to build a CV that adapts to UK, US, Canadian, Gulf-region, European, or Asian formats, and export each version as a clean, ATS-friendly PDF for free. The data lives in your browser; save it as JSON to back it up or move it between devices.

The most important international CV differences are: photo conventions (US/UK/Canada omit, UAE/Germany/France often include), length conventions (US/Canada prefer one page, UK/Europe prefer two, Gulf region accepts three), personal details (US/UK/Canada omit age and marital status, Gulf/Europe/Asia often include), and language conventions (British vs American English, regional terminology).

When applying for roles in multiple countries, build a master version with all your information, then create a tailored export for each market. Save each version as a separate JSON file so you can re-import and update them independently. This is far more efficient than rebuilding from scratch for each application.

ATS compatibility varies by country. US and Canadian ATS platforms are the most strict — single-column layouts only. UK and European platforms are slightly more forgiving. Language matters more than people realise. A CV written in British English will read as foreign to a US recruiter, and vice versa. Mirror the language and spelling conventions of the country you are applying to. What to include

Adaptable contact details

Phone number with country code, professional email, and current city and country. Add or remove a full address based on local conventions — US/UK/Canada omit, Gulf/Europe often include. Include LinkedIn URL if your profile is polished.

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Adjust this for each country — emphasise different skills or experiences based on what local recruiters value. Avoid clichés; specificity wins in every market.

Work experience in reverse chronological order

Most recent role first. Each entry should include job title, employer, dates (month and year), location, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Quantify outcomes wherever possible.

Education with relevant detail

Degree, institution, and graduation year. Add coursework, honours, or capstone projects for recent graduates. Mention degree equivalence if your qualification is from a different country (e.g., "Recognised as equivalent to a UK Master's degree by NARIC").

Skills grouped by category

Technical skills, soft skills, and languages. Languages are particularly valuable for international roles — list every language you can use professionally with a proficiency level. Mirror the terminology of the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match.

Optional sections tailored to the market

Photos (UAE/Germany/France yes; US/UK/Canada no), personal details (UAE/Asia yes; US/UK/Canada no), volunteer experience (Canada/US valued; Europe less so). Toggle sections based on local conventions.

Tips for a stronger resume

Research the country's CV conventions before applying

Each country has specific expectations around length, photo, personal details, and structure. Spend ten minutes researching before you send — a culturally fluent CV signals respect and increases your chances. The internet is full of country-specific CV guides.

Build a master version, then tailor per country

Create a comprehensive master CV with all your information. For each country application, duplicate the file and adjust: photo on/off, personal details in/out, length, language conventions. Save each version as a separate JSON file.

Mirror the language and spelling of the target country

British English for UK/Ireland/Commonwealth, American English for US/Canada/Philippines, local language for non-English-speaking countries. Mixing conventions reads as careless. Have a native speaker review if you are applying in a non-native language.

Mention degree equivalence if your qualification is from abroad

If you studied in one country and are applying in another, mention degree equivalence (e.g., "Recognised as equivalent to a UK Master's degree by NARIC"). This saves the recruiter from having to look it up and signals that you have done your homework.

Highlight international experience

If you have studied, worked, or volunteered abroad, highlight it prominently. International experience demonstrates adaptability, cultural awareness, and language capability — all valuable for international roles. Burying it in your work history is a missed opportunity.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

ATS conventions vary by country, but the single-column ATS layout is the safest format everywhere. Save the styled version for direct emails and networking events. The same data, two export formats, no rebuilding required.

Have a native speaker review your CV

If you are applying in a non-native language, have a native speaker review your CV before sending. They will catch phrasing that sounds odd, terminology that is regionally wrong, and cultural references that do not translate. This is the single best investment you can make.

Common mistakes to avoid

Using the same CV for every country

A US-style resume sent to a UAE employer reads as thin (no photo, no personal details). A UAE-style CV sent to a US employer reads as unprofessional (photo, age, marital status). Tailor for each market — the same data, different framing.

Mixing British and American English

"Organisation" and "organization" in the same document signals carelessness. Pick one convention (based on the country you are applying to) and use it consistently. Run a spellcheck with the correct dictionary selected.

Forgetting to translate or localise

If you are applying in a non-English-speaking country, translate your CV into the local language. If you are applying in English to a non-English-speaking country, use clear international English (avoid idioms, slang, or cultural references that may not translate).

Including the wrong personal details for the market

US/UK/Canada: omit photo, age, marital status, religion, national ID. Gulf region: include photo, nationality, visa status. Germany/France: photo sometimes included, personal details sometimes included. Research the conventions for each country.

Using a one-size-fits-all length

US/Canada: one to two pages. UK/Europe: two pages. Gulf region: two to three pages. Academia: three to five pages. Pick the right length for the market. A one-page CV sent to a UAE employer reads as under-prepared; a three-page CV sent to a US employer reads as self-indulgent.
