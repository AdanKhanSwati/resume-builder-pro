The distinction between a CV and a resume matters depending on where you are applying. What to include

A clear personal statement at the top

Two or three lines that summarise who you are, what you do, and what you are looking for. Avoid clichés like "hardworking team player" — use specific, evidence-backed language instead.

Reverse-chronological work history

Start with your most recent role and work backwards. For each position, include your title, the employer, dates (month and year), and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Older roles can be compressed to a single line.

Education and qualifications

List your highest qualification first, with institution and dates. Add professional qualifications (ACCA, PMP, CFA) in a separate Certifications section if you have several — they are too important to bury inside Education.

A focused skills inventory

Group your skills into categories — technical, soft, languages, tools. Mirror the terminology used in your target job description, but never list a skill you cannot confidently demonstrate in an interview.

Quantified achievements

Where possible, include a separate Achievements section for awards, published work, or notable outcomes. Use the bullet format in your work history for everyday accomplishments; reserve this section for the highlights.

Optional supporting sections

Languages (with proficiency level), volunteer experience, projects, and interests all add colour when used selectively. Enable only what strengthens your case for the specific role you are targeting.

Tips for a stronger resume

Pick a format that matches your region

UK and European CVs run one to two pages with no photo. US resumes are strictly one page for early-career applicants. Gulf-region CVs often include a photo and personal details. Match the local convention to avoid standing out for the wrong reason.

Mirror the job description's language

If the posting asks for "stakeholder management", use that exact phrase rather than "client relations". ATS systems match on terminology, and human recruiters scan for familiar terms. Just do not overdo it — natural variation reads better than robotic repetition.

Lead with achievements, not responsibilities

"Responsible for managing a team of eight" describes the job spec. "Built and retained a team of eight that delivered 23% year-on-year revenue growth" describes what you actually did. The second version is what gets you the interview.

Use white space deliberately

Cramped documents signal desperation — like you are trying to hide a thin background behind dense text. Confident applicants use generous margins, clear section breaks, and breathing room around each entry.

Save the master version, tailor the export

Keep one comprehensive document with everything you have ever done. For each application, duplicate the file and trim it to the most relevant 80%. This is faster than rebuilding from scratch each time and ensures you never forget an important detail.

Test the PDF on a phone

Many recruiters review applications on mobile during their commute. If your CV is unreadable on a 6-inch screen, you have lost an opportunity before the interview is even scheduled. The live preview in this builder shows you exactly what recruiters will see.

Name the file professionally

Save as "Jane_Doe_CV_Marketing_Manager.pdf" — not "cv_final_v3.pdf". The filename shows up in the recruiter's downloads folder and is often the first impression you make.

Common mistakes to avoid

Writing in the third person

"Jane is a results-driven marketer" reads as stiff and dated. Use first person with the pronouns dropped: "Results-driven marketer with eight years of B2B SaaS experience". That is the modern convention in every English-speaking market.

Including irrelevant personal details

Age, marital status, religion, and national ID numbers have no place on a modern CV in most countries. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact information and professional details.

Going over two pages without cause

Senior hires with 15+ years of experience can justify a two-page CV. Anyone else should aim for one. If you are struggling to fit, cut the oldest roles, drop the interests section, and tighten your bullet points.

Using a generic template

Every applicant has access to the same free templates. If yours looks identical to fifty others in the pile, you have not differentiated yourself. Use the modular layout in this builder and adjust the order, headings, and section emphasis to fit your story.

Forgetting to update dates

A CV with end dates from three years ago and no current role suggests you are out of work — whether true or not. Use "Present" for your current position, and review the document every six months to keep it current.
