What to include

A targeted professional summary

Two or three lines that position you for the role. Mention your degree, your strongest skill area, and one achievement. Avoid generic objective statements — recruiters stopped reading those years ago. Update this for every application.

Education with relevant detail

Degree, institution, dates, GPA (if 3.5+), relevant coursework, and any academic honours. For entry-level applicants, Education is usually the strongest section — give it space at the top of the document.

Any work experience, internships, or part-time roles

Frame each role around transferable skills and quantified outcomes. Even unrelated work demonstrates reliability, time management, and customer service. Treat every role with the same care, however junior.

Skills grouped by category

Technical skills (software, programming languages, tools), soft skills (communication, leadership, problem-solving), and languages (with proficiency level). Mirror the terminology from the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match.

Projects, both academic and personal

Treat each significant project as a work experience entry. List the project name, your role, the tools used, and the outcome. Include links to live projects, GitHub repositories, or online portfolios where possible.

Certifications and online courses

AWS, Google, Coursera, or edX certifications show self-directed learning beyond your degree. List them with the issuing organisation and completion date. These are particularly valuable at the entry level — they demonstrate initiative and capability.

Tips for a stronger resume

Lead with your strongest section

For most entry-level applicants, that means Education at the top. If you have an impressive internship, lead with Work Experience. If you have stellar projects, lead with Projects. The first section the recruiter sees should be your best selling point.

Quantify your achievements

"Top 10% of cohort", "Selected from 80 applicants", "Raised £400 for charity", "Captain of university team (12-2 season)". Numbers make achievements concrete and comparable across candidates, regardless of work history.

Mirror the language of the job description

If the posting asks for "stakeholder management", use that phrase. ATS systems match on terminology, and human recruiters scan for familiar terms. Just ensure the usage is natural — keyword stuffing is detected and penalised.

Keep it to one page

Entry-level documents should never exceed one page. You do not have enough material to justify two. Be ruthless about cutting irrelevant coursework, unrelated hobbies, and outdated achievements.

Use the AI assist to strengthen your bullet points

Most entry-level applicants write vague bullets. The AI tool can rewrite "worked on a team project" as "Led a four-person team to ship a React-based web app with 95 Lighthouse score". Accept the suggestions that fit, reject those that do not.

Tailor for each application

A consulting role wants analytical thinking evidence. A tech role wants technical depth. A charity wants commitment to a cause. Adjust your summary, skills, and section order for each application — five tailored versions beat fifty generic ones.

Get feedback from your careers service

Most universities and many community organisations offer free CV reviews. Use them. A fresh pair of eyes catches typos, awkward phrasing, and missing sections you have gone blind to after staring at the document.

Common mistakes to avoid

Apologising for lack of experience

Never write "although I have no experience" or "I know I am just a beginner". Recruiters know you are entry-level — they advertised an entry-level role. Focus on what you do bring, not what you lack.

Using a generic template without customisation

Templates are a starting point, not a finished document. Every section should be tailored to your specific story and the role you are applying for. A copy-paste template with placeholder text still visible is worse than no template at all.

Listing irrelevant coursework

Include only courses relevant to the role. A recruiter scanning your document does not need to see your first-year electives. Five well-chosen courses beat a comprehensive list of twenty.

Including high school achievements

Once you have a university degree, high school is generally irrelevant. Drop it entirely unless you are applying for a role that specifically asks for it. The space is better used for university-level work and projects.

Sending the same document to every employer

Entry-level roles vary enormously in what they look for. Tailor your summary, skills, and section order for each application — five tailored versions beat fifty generic ones. The template makes tailoring fast, so use that speed to your advantage.
