Your first application document sets the tone for your job search, and it is normal to feel unsure about what to include when you have never had a job before. This builder is designed to make that first attempt straightforward, structured, and confidence-inspiring.

Instead of leaving you to guess at formatting and structure, the tool walks you through each section — contact details, education, skills, and any experience you do have, including school projects or volunteer work — so nothing important gets left out. Every section comes with hints and examples to help you write in the conventions recruiters expect.

The result is a clean, professional document that presents you confidently, ready to download and start sending out to employers today. Even if your work history is zero, you have material to draw on — school projects, sports team participation, volunteer work, hobbies that demonstrate discipline, and skills you have picked up along the way.

First-job applications are reviewed differently than experienced-hire applications. Recruiters hiring for entry-level roles know they are not getting polished candidates with decades of experience. They are looking for potential, reliability, and a willingness to learn. Your document should signal all three without apologising for what you do not yet have.

The biggest mistake first-time applicants make is underselling non-work experience. A paper round shows reliability and time management. A Saturday retail job shows customer service. A school coding club shows technical interest. Captaining a sports team shows leadership. Frame these experiences with the same care you would give to a paid role — the transferable skills are what employers are buying.

If you genuinely have no work, volunteer, or extracurricular experience, lean on your education and any personal projects. A self-taught coder with a GitHub portfolio can land a junior developer role without ever having had a formal job. A self-published blog demonstrates writing ability. A personal art portfolio demonstrates creative skill. The world is full of evidence you can produce — you just have to look for it.

Once you have built your document, you can tailor it for each application in minutes. Adjust the summary, swap the order of your strengths, and mirror the language of the job posting. The first job is the hardest to land — once you have it, every subsequent application becomes easier because you have real experience to draw on.

What to include

Contact details that actually work

Professional email (not a childhood nickname), phone number you check daily, and your city. Add LinkedIn if your profile is set up. Skip the full street address — city and postcode are enough for a first job.

Education at the top

For first-job applicants, Education is usually the strongest section. Include your most recent qualification, institution, and dates. Add coursework, projects, or honours relevant to the role you are applying for.

Any work experience, however small

Paper round, babysitting, Saturday job, summer work — all of it counts. Frame each role around transferable skills: reliability, time management, customer service, teamwork. The role itself matters less than what you learned from it.

School projects and personal work

Treat significant school projects like work experience entries. List the project, your role, the tools used, and the outcome. The same applies to personal projects — a YouTube channel, a blog, a coding portfolio, an Etsy shop.

Extracurricular activities

Sports teams, drama clubs, debate societies, music groups — all demonstrate commitment, teamwork, and discipline. Include one or two of the most relevant. Captaincy or leadership roles are particularly valuable.

Skills section

List technical skills (software, programming languages, tools) and soft skills (communication, organisation, problem-solving). Mirror the language of the job posting so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match.

Tips for a stronger resume

Frame non-work experience like work experience

A Saturday retail job is not "just a part-time thing". It is customer service experience, cash handling, stock management, and teamwork. Treat each role — however small — with the same seriousness you would a full-time position.

Lead with your strongest section

If your grades are strong, lead with Education. If you have an impressive project, lead with Projects. If you have a part-time job relevant to the application, lead with Work Experience. Put your best foot forward — first impressions matter.

Quantify wherever you can

"Member of school debate team that reached regional finals", "Captain of under-16 football team (12-2 season)", "Raised £400 for charity through bake sale organising". Numbers make achievements concrete and memorable.

Keep it to one page

First-job documents should never exceed one page. You do not have enough material to justify two. Be ruthless about cutting irrelevant detail and outdated achievements. Quality over quantity, always.

Get a teacher or parent to read it

A fresh pair of eyes catches typos, awkward phrasing, and missing information. Ask a teacher, parent, or older sibling to review your document before you send it. Five minutes of feedback can save you from embarrassing mistakes.

Apply widely but thoughtfully

For your first job, expect to send many applications before you get an interview. Tailor each one — a generic mass mailing is rarely effective. Twenty tailored applications beat two hundred copy-pasted ones.

Follow up politely

If you have not heard back after two weeks, a polite email asking about the status of your application shows initiative. Many first-job offers go to candidates who followed up — it demonstrates genuine interest in the role.

Common mistakes to avoid

Apologising for lack of experience

Never write "although I have no experience" or "I know I am just a beginner". Recruiters know you are entry-level — they advertised an entry-level role. Focus on what you do bring, not what you lack.

Including irrelevant personal details

Date of birth, marital status, religion, and national ID numbers are not needed in most markets. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact details and qualifications.

Listing hobbies that add no value

"Reading, music, and socialising" tells the recruiter nothing. Either describe hobbies that demonstrate relevant skills ("competitive chess — regional under-18 champion") or drop the section entirely. Generic interests waste space.

Using a childish email address

"skaterdude99@email.com" destroys your credibility before the recruiter reads a word. Set up a simple "firstname.lastname" email address — it takes two minutes and signals maturity.

Sending the same document to every employer

A retail role wants customer service evidence. An office junior role wants organisation and IT skills. An apprenticeship wants technical aptitude. Adjust your summary and section order for each application — five tailored versions beat fifty generic ones.

Frequently Asked Questions

I have never had a job — can I still make a document?

Yes. Focus on education, skills, and any projects, volunteer work, or extracurricular activities you have been part of. The builder lets you disable the Work Experience section entirely and lean on Projects, Volunteer Experience, and Achievements instead.

Can I download my first document as a PDF?

Yes, you can export your finished document as a PDF instantly. PDF is the recommended format for job applications because it preserves your formatting across every device and operating system.

Will recruiters take a first-job document seriously?

Yes — a clear, well-organised document that highlights your potential is exactly what entry-level employers look for. They know they are not hiring experienced candidates; they want evidence of reliability, initiative, and willingness to learn.

Should I include my date of birth?

In most markets, no — date of birth is not needed and can expose employers to age discrimination claims. Include it only if the role specifically requires it (some apprenticeship programmes, for example).

Can I include high school achievements?

Yes, if you are still in school or recently graduated. As you gain work experience, high school achievements should be dropped in favour of more recent qualifications. For a first job, they are perfectly appropriate.

What if I have only had one part-time job?

That is fine — one well-described role is better than three vague ones. Frame it carefully: list your responsibilities, any training you received, and any recognition you got (employee of the month, positive feedback from a manager).
