What to include

Contact information that actually reaches you

Use a professional email address (not a nickname), a phone number you check daily, and a city and country rather than a full street address. Add a LinkedIn URL if your profile is polished, and a portfolio or GitHub link if your work can be viewed online.

A short professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role you want — not the role you have. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area, and one quantified achievement. Skip the objective statement; recruiters stopped reading those a decade ago.

Work experience with measurable results

For each role, list your job title, employer, dates, and three to five bullet points. Each bullet should describe an achievement with a metric — percent improvement, dollars saved, hours reduced, users acquired — rather than a generic responsibility.

Education with relevant detail

Include degree, institution, and graduation year. If you graduated within the last three years, add coursework, honours, or a capstone project. Experienced hires can keep this section to a single line per degree.

A targeted skills section

List technical and soft skills as short tags grouped by category. Mirror the language of the job description so ATS software recognises the match, but only include skills you can defend in an interview.

Optional sections that add depth

Projects, certifications, languages, volunteer experience, and achievements each have a dedicated module. Enable only the ones that strengthen your application for the specific role you are targeting.

Tips for a stronger resume

Tailor every application

A generic document sent to fifty employers is less effective than five tailored versions. Adjust your summary, skills, and bullet points to mirror the language of each job description. Ten minutes of customisation beats ten hours of mass applications.

Quantify wherever possible

Numbers jump off the page. "Led a team of six" is stronger than "Led a team". "Cut monthly reporting time by 40%" is stronger than "Improved reporting efficiency". If you cannot measure it, describe the scope — team size, budget, user count, project duration.

Start every bullet with an action verb

"Spearheaded", "engineered", "shipped", "negotiated", "streamlined" — strong verbs carry more weight than "was responsible for" or "helped with". Vary the verbs across bullets to keep the section readable.

Keep it to one page if you have under ten years of experience

Two pages is acceptable for senior hires with extensive history, but recruiters rarely read past the first page. Be ruthless about cutting outdated roles, irrelevant coursework, and hobbies that do not reinforce your professional narrative.

Save as PDF unless asked otherwise

PDFs preserve formatting across every device and operating system. DOCX files can render differently depending on the recruiter's version of Word. Only send a DOCX if the employer explicitly requests an editable file.

Use a clean, web-safe font in 10-12pt

Arial, Calibri, Helvetica, or Georgia are safe choices. Avoid decorative fonts, multi-colour text, or graphics that distract from the content. Readability beats novelty every time.

Run a final consistency pass

Check that every date format matches, every bullet ends the same way (with or without a period — pick one), and every employer name is spelled out consistently. Small inconsistencies signal carelessness to a hiring manager.

Common mistakes to avoid

Including a photo on US, UK, or Canadian applications

In these markets, photos are discouraged because they expose employers to bias claims. Many ATS platforms strip images anyway, which can corrupt your layout. Save the headshot for your LinkedIn profile.

Stuffing keywords from the job description

A natural scattering of relevant terms helps; a white-text block of every keyword in the posting gets your application flagged and rejected. Write for a human reader first, then verify the key terms appear once or twice.

Using tables or columns for layout

Multi-column layouts confuse older ATS parsers — they read left-to-right, top-to-bottom, and often jumble the content. Stick to a single-column structure for applications going through online portals.

Listing references directly on the page

Recruiters ask for references later in the process. Use the line "References available on request" or omit the section entirely. Including contact details for your referees without warning them is also poor etiquette.

Typos and grammatical errors

A single typo can drop your application to the bottom of the pile, especially for roles that involve written communication. Run a spellcheck, read it out loud once, and ask a friend to scan it. The free builder includes a live preview so mistakes are easier to spot.
