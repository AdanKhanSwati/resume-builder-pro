What to include

Education at the top

For applicants without work experience, Education is your strongest section. Include degree, institution, dates, GPA (if 3.5+), relevant coursework, and any academic honours. Lead with this section — it is your best selling point.

Projects, both academic and personal

Treat each significant project as a work experience entry. List the project name, your role, the tools used, and the outcome. Include links to live projects, GitHub repositories, or online portfolios where possible — proof beats claims.

Skills grouped by category

Technical skills (software, programming languages, tools), soft skills (communication, leadership, problem-solving), and languages (with proficiency level). Mirror the language of the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match.

Volunteer experience

Treat volunteer roles like work experience entries. List the role, organisation, dates, and impact. Volunteering demonstrates initiative, commitment, and community engagement — all qualities employers value, especially in entry-level candidates.

Extracurricular activities and leadership

Society committee roles, sports captaincy, event organising, or club leadership all demonstrate soft skills. Include one or two of the most relevant — quality beats quantity. Focus on roles where you had responsibility or made an impact.

Certifications and online courses

AWS, Google, Coursera, or edX certifications show self-directed learning. List them with the issuing organisation and completion date. These are particularly valuable when you have no formal work experience — they show initiative and capability.

Tips for a stronger resume

Lead with your strongest section

Without work experience, lead with Education, Projects, or Skills — whichever is strongest for the role you are applying for. Put your best foot forward; do not start with an apologetic summary about your lack of experience.

Treat projects like work experience

Academic projects, personal projects, and side hustles all count. Describe them with the same care you would a paid role: project name, your role, the tools used, the outcome, and any metrics. Three concrete projects beat ten vague bullet points.

Quantify your achievements

"Achieved 94% on capstone project", "Selected from 80 applicants for funded research", "Raised £400 for charity through event organising". Numbers make achievements concrete and comparable across candidates, regardless of work history.

Use the AI assist to strengthen your language

Most first-time applicants write vague, passive bullets. The AI tool can rewrite "worked on a team project" as "Led a four-person team to ship a React-based web app with 95 Lighthouse score". Accept the suggestions that fit, reject those that do not.

Mirror the language of the job description

If the posting asks for "customer service skills", use that exact phrase. ATS systems match on terminology, and human recruiters scan for familiar terms. Just ensure the usage is natural — keyword stuffing is detected and penalised.

Include links to your work

GitHub for code, Behance or Dribbble for design, a personal website for writing. Live links to actual work beat claims on paper. Make sure the repositories and portfolios are polished — recruiters will click through.

Keep it to one page

No-experience documents should never exceed one page. You do not have enough material to justify two. Be ruthless about cutting irrelevant coursework, unrelated hobbies, and outdated achievements.

Common mistakes to avoid

Apologising for lack of experience

Never write "although I have no experience" or "I know I am just a beginner". Recruiters know you are entry-level — they advertised an entry-level role. Focus on what you do bring, not what you lack.

Leaving the Work Experience section empty

An empty section draws attention to what is missing. Disable the Work Experience section entirely and use Projects, Volunteer Experience, or Achievements instead. The layout reflows to fill the space naturally.

Listing irrelevant coursework

Include only courses relevant to the role you are applying for. A recruiter scanning your document does not need to see your first-year electives. Five well-chosen courses beat a comprehensive list of twenty.

Using vague, passive language

"Worked on a team project" tells the recruiter nothing. "Led a four-person team to ship a React-based web app with 95 Lighthouse score" tells them exactly what you did. Specificity and active verbs win, especially when you have no work history to fall back on.

Overusing buzzwords

"Passionate", "hardworking", "team player", "results-driven" — recruiters read these words dozens of times per document. Show these qualities through your achievements instead of claiming them. Strong verbs and quantified outcomes beat adjectives.
