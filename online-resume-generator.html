What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three lines that position you for the role: years of experience, core specialism, and one quantified outcome. Update this for every application — a generic summary is a wasted opportunity.

Work experience in reverse chronological order

Most recent role first. Each entry should have title, employer, dates, location, and achievement-focused bullets. Compress older roles into one or two lines once you have 10+ years of history.

Education tailored to your career stage

Recent graduates: include coursework, honours, and capstone projects. Experienced hires: list degree, institution, and year only. Doctoral holders: include dissertation title and advisor if relevant to the role.

Skills that match the job posting

Group by category — technical, soft, languages, tools. Mirror the exact terminology used in the job description, but only include skills you can defend in an interview. Five well-chosen tags beat twenty generic ones.

Projects or portfolio links

For technical and creative roles, link to live projects, GitHub repositories, or online portfolios. Three concrete examples with measurable outcomes are more compelling than a long list of undifferentiated work.

Certifications and credentials

List professional certifications (PMP, AWS, CPA, CFA) with issuer and date. For technical roles, include cloud platform certifications and framework-specific credentials. Drop expired certifications unless they remain industry-relevant.

Tips for a stronger resume

Generate a master version, then tailor per application

Build a comprehensive document with every role, project, and skill you have ever had. Save it as JSON. For each application, duplicate the file, trim to the most relevant 80%, and adjust the summary and skills sections to mirror the job description.

Use the live preview to catch formatting issues

The preview pane updates instantly as you type. Use it to spot orphan lines, broken bullet points, and awkward page breaks. A document that looks polished in the preview will look polished when the recruiter opens it.

Generate a fresh version for each major application

Recruiters can tell when you have sent the same generic document to fifty employers. Spend ten minutes customising your summary, skills, and top bullet points for each role — the response rate jumps measurably.

Switch to ATS Mode for online portals

When applying through Workday, Greenhouse, or iCIMS, switch on ATS Mode. The simplified single-column layout parses more reliably and reduces the chance of your information being jumbled or dropped.

Save your data as JSON for backup

Use the Save JSON button regularly. If your browser storage is cleared, you can re-import your data and pick up where you left off. The JSON file is small enough to email to yourself or store in any cloud drive.

Review the generated PDF on a phone

Many recruiters first review applications on mobile. Open your exported PDF on your phone and check that the text is readable, the layout holds together, and there are no awkward breaks across pages.

Run a final keyword check against the job posting

After generating, scan your document for the five to ten most important terms from the job description. If any are missing, work them in naturally. The generator makes this easy — just edit and re-export.

Common mistakes to avoid

Relying on the generator to do the writing

The tool structures your content; it does not invent it. Vague bullets like "worked on various projects" will not become strong bullets just because they appear in a clean layout. Spend time on the writing — the generator amplifies good content, not bad.

Exporting before checking the preview

The live preview exists for a reason. Take thirty seconds to scan it before clicking export. Look for empty sections, broken dates, or overflowing bullet points that wrap awkwardly across lines.

Sending the same file to every employer

A generic document is the single biggest reason applications get rejected. The generator makes tailoring fast — use that speed to your advantage. Five tailored versions beat fifty generic ones.

Forgetting to update the summary

Your summary is the first thing a recruiter reads. If it positions you as a "customer success specialist" but you are applying for a "solutions architect" role, the application is dead on arrival. Rewrite the summary for every application.

Choosing style over ATS compatibility

A two-column layout with a sidebar looks beautiful, but many ATS platforms cannot parse it. For online applications, always use ATS Mode. Save the styled version for direct emails and networking.

No signup is required — you can start generating immediately. Your data is saved in your browser's localStorage, so you can return to the same device and continue editing without an account.

Yes. The editor preserves your data, so you can return anytime, make changes, and re-export. If you exported as DOCX, you can also edit the file directly in Microsoft Word or Google Docs.
