What to include

Industry-specific language and terminology

Mirror the language of the job description and the industry. Tech resumes reference frameworks and cloud platforms. Finance resumes reference accounting standards and software. Marketing resumes reference channels and metrics. Use the right vocabulary for your field.

Quantified achievements in every bullet

Strong examples always quantify outcomes. "Increased sales by 23%", "managed team of 12", "shipped feature to 200K users". Numbers make achievements concrete and comparable across candidates. Without numbers, your bullets are just responsibility lists.

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area, and one quantified achievement. Update this for every application — a generic summary is a wasted opportunity.

Reverse-chronological work experience

Most recent role first. Each entry should include title, employer, dates, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Compress older roles once you have 10+ years of history. The reverse-chronological format is what recruiters expect.

A focused skills section

Group skills by category. Mirror the terminology of the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match. Five well-chosen skills beat twenty generic ones. Only include skills you can defend in an interview.

Consistent formatting throughout

Pick one date format, one bullet style, and one font. Be consistent across every section. Inconsistencies signal carelessness. Strong examples always look polished — the formatting is invisible because it is consistent.

Tips for a stronger resume

Study examples from your specific industry

Tech examples differ from finance examples, which differ from marketing examples. Look at examples from your specific field to learn the language, metrics, and emphasis recruiters expect. Generic examples are less useful than industry-specific ones.

Pay attention to how bullets quantify outcomes

The single biggest difference between strong and weak resumes is quantification. "Increased sales by 23%" beats "responsible for sales". Study how strong examples quantify outcomes — and apply the same pattern to your own bullets.

Adapt patterns, do not copy verbatim

Use examples as inspiration for structure and language, not templates to copy word-for-word. Your resume needs to reflect your specific experience. Copying another applicant's language verbatim is dishonest and usually obvious to recruiters.

Look at examples one level above your target role

If you are applying for a mid-level role, look at senior-level examples to see what language and patterns recruiters expect from candidates ready to step up. Mirror that language in your own document — but be honest about what you have actually done.

Use the AI assist to apply patterns to your own bullets

After studying examples, use the AI assist tool in the builder to apply the same patterns to your own bullets. Accept the suggestions that fit, reject those that do not. You stay in control of every edit.

Test your finished resume against the patterns

After building your document, compare it to the examples. Does your summary position you clearly? Do your bullets quantify outcomes? Does your skills section mirror the job description? If not, iterate until it does.

Get feedback from someone in your industry

Show your finished resume to someone in your industry — ideally someone who has hired for similar roles. They will catch industry-specific issues that a general reviewer would miss. Five minutes of feedback from the right person is worth hours of independent editing.

Common mistakes to avoid

Copying example language verbatim

Use examples as inspiration, not templates to copy word-for-word. Your resume needs to reflect your specific experience. Copying another applicant's language verbatim is dishonest and usually obvious to recruiters who have seen the same examples online.

Following examples from a different industry

Tech examples differ from finance examples, which differ from marketing examples. Following an example from the wrong industry gives you the wrong language, metrics, and emphasis. Always study examples from your specific field.

Ignoring the patterns that make examples work

Strong examples share common patterns: quantified outcomes, reverse-chronological structure, targeted summaries, focused skills sections. If you only copy the surface (headings, font, layout) without applying the underlying patterns, your resume will not improve.

Treating examples as a ceiling rather than a floor

Examples show you what a competent resume looks like. They are not the upper limit. Use them as a starting point, then push further — stronger quantification, more targeted language, more focused content. The best resumes exceed the examples.

Using outdated examples

Resume conventions change over time. Objective statements were common in the 2000s but are now obsolete. Multi-column layouts were popular briefly but break ATS parsing. Make sure the examples you study are recent — within the last two to three years.
