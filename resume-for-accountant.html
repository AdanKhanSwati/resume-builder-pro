What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area (audit, tax, FP&A, etc.), your key certification (CPA, ACCA, CMA), and one quantified achievement. Avoid generic statements like "detail-oriented accountant" — every applicant says that.

Work experience with quantified outcomes

Each role should include title, employer, dates (month and year), and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Quantify outcomes wherever possible — "managed month-end close in 5 days instead of 10", "identified $2M in tax savings", "reduced accounts receivable days by 23%".

Education with relevant detail

Degree, institution, and graduation year. For accountants, professional qualifications (ACCA, CPA, CMA, CA, CFA) are often as important as the degree itself. List these in a Certifications section with the issuing body and completion year.

Certifications and licences

CPA, ACCA, CMA, CA, CFA, CIMA — list every relevant certification with the issuing body and completion year. Mention if your certification is current and in good standing. Include membership numbers if relevant. Drop expired certifications unless still widely recognised.

Accounting software skills

List the specific platforms you have used — SAP, Oracle, QuickBooks, Xero, Sage, NetSuite, Dynamics 365 — and your level of expertise with each. Generic claims like "experienced with accounting software" tell the recruiter nothing. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description.

Technical accounting skills

Group your technical skills by category: financial reporting, audit, tax, FP&A, accounts payable/receivable, payroll, reconciliation, GAAP/IFRS expertise. Mirror the terminology of the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match.

Tips for a stronger resume

Quantify your accounting impact

Every bullet should have a metric. "Managed month-end close in 5 days instead of 10", "identified $2M in tax savings through R&D credit analysis", "reduced accounts receivable days by 23% through process improvement". Numbers make accounting work concrete and comparable across candidates.

List certifications prominently

CPA, ACCA, CMA, CA, CFA, CIMA — these are critical differentiators in the accounting job market. List them in a Certifications section near the top of your document. Mention if your certification is current and in good standing. Include membership numbers if relevant.

Be specific about accounting software

List the exact platforms you have used — SAP, Oracle, QuickBooks, Xero, Sage, NetSuite, Dynamics 365 — and your level of expertise with each. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match. Generic claims like "experienced with accounting software" tell the recruiter nothing.

Highlight GAAP or IFRS expertise

If you have experience with specific accounting standards (US GAAP, IFRS, UK GAAP), mention it explicitly. This is particularly important for multinational roles. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

Workday, Taleo, Greenhouse, and iCIMS are widely used in the finance sector. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct recruiter emails or networking events.

Include audit or regulatory experience

If you have audit experience (internal, external, or regulatory), mention it explicitly. Experience with SOX compliance, internal controls, or regulatory reporting (SEC, FCA, SFC) is highly valued. Quantify the scope of audits you have worked on — team size, entity size, complexity.

Mention process improvements

Accountants who can identify and implement process improvements are highly valued. If you have automated a manual process, reduced close time, or implemented a new system, mention it explicitly with measurable outcomes. This separates you from accountants who only execute existing processes.

Common mistakes to avoid

Listing generic responsibilities instead of achievements

"Responsible for accounts payable" tells the recruiter nothing. "Processed 500+ invoices monthly with 99.7% accuracy, reduced average processing time from 5 days to 2 days through workflow automation" tells them exactly what you did. Quantify outcomes wherever possible.

Omitting certifications or software experience

CPA, ACCA, CMA, and accounting software experience (SAP, Oracle, QuickBooks, Xero) are critical differentiators in the accounting job market. List them prominently. Omitting them forces the recruiter to guess — and they will usually guess wrong.

Vague date ranges or unexplained gaps

Finance recruiters pay particular attention to dates. Make sure every role has clear start and end dates (month and year). Unexplained gaps raise questions — address them briefly in your cover letter if necessary. Frequent job changes (more than three roles in five years) also raise questions.

Going over two pages

Accounting resumes should be one to two pages. One page for entry-level accountants; two pages for experienced hires. Three pages is acceptable only at the controller or CFO level with extensive history. Finance recruiters will not read past the second page.

Including irrelevant personal details

Date of birth, marital status, religion, and national ID numbers have no place on a modern accounting resume. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact details and professional information.
