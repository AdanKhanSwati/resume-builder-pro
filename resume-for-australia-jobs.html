Australian resumes are typically two to three pages, even for mid-career applicants. One page is acceptable only for very entry-level roles or recent graduates. The longer format reflects Australian recruiters' expectation of comprehensive work history with detailed achievement bullets.

Australian English follows British spelling conventions ("organisation", "specialise", "colour") but has some unique vocabulary ("university" rather than "college", "CV" and "resume" used interchangeably, "references" often called "referees"). Mirror the language of the job posting and use Australian conventions consistently throughout.

ATS usage is widespread in Australia, particularly among large employers, government departments, and the financial sector. Workday, PageUp, Taleo, and Greenhouse are all commonly used. Right to work in Australia is a critical consideration. Australian citizens and permanent residents do not need to state their status. If you are on a visa (Working Holiday, Skilled Migration, Employer Nomination), mention it briefly if you are comfortable — Australian employers are generally familiar with the visa system and may be willing to sponsor the right candidate.

Australian recruiters value cultural fit highly. The job interview often includes behavioural questions designed to assess how you work in a team, handle conflict, and adapt to change. Your resume should signal these qualities through achievement-focused bullets that highlight collaboration, leadership, and adaptability — not just technical capability.

What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area, and one quantified achievement. Australian recruiters expect a summary that sells what you bring, not what you want.

Work experience in reverse chronological order

Most recent role first. Each entry should include job title, employer, dates (month and year), location, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Quantify outcomes wherever possible.

Education and certifications

Degree, institution, and graduation year. Australian recruiters also value professional certifications (CA ANZ, CPA Australia, AACA, Engineers Australia) and vocational qualifications (TAFE, VET). List these in a Certifications section if you have several.

Skills section with technical and soft skills

Group skills by category. Mirror the terminology used in Australian job postings. If the role requires specific Australian certifications or clearances (Baselines, NV1), mention them explicitly.

Visa status (if not an Australian citizen)

If you are on a Working Holiday visa, Skilled Migration visa, or Employer Nomination Scheme visa, mention it briefly. Australian employers are generally familiar with the visa system and may be willing to sponsor the right candidate. Honesty saves everyone time.

Referees (not references)

Australian convention is to list referees (not references) on a separate page or to write "Referees available on request". Do not include referee contact details directly on your main resume — recruiters will ask for them later in the process.

Tips for a stronger resume

Use Australian English spelling

"Organisation" not "organization", "specialise" not "specialize", "colour" not "color". Mixing British and American spelling signals carelessness. Run a spellcheck with the Australian English dictionary selected.

Keep it to two to three pages

Australian resumes are typically longer than US versions. Two pages is standard for mid-career applicants. Three pages is acceptable for senior hires. One page is acceptable only for very entry-level roles or recent graduates.

Quantify your achievements

Australian recruiters expect measurable results. "Increased sales by 23%", "managed a team of 12", "shipped product to 50,000 users". Numbers make achievements concrete and comparable across candidates.

Mention visa status if relevant

If you are not an Australian citizen, mention your visa status briefly. Australian employers are generally familiar with the visa system. Honesty saves everyone time and avoids awkward conversations late in the recruitment process.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

Workday, PageUp, Taleo, and Greenhouse are widely used in Australia. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct emails to recruiters or networking events.

Use Australian terminology

"University" not "college", "CV" and "resume" are interchangeable, "referees" not "references". Mirror the language of the job posting. Consistency matters more than which convention you choose.

Highlight Australian experience

If you have worked in Australia, highlight it prominently. Local experience is highly valued by Australian recruiters and reduces perceived risk for the employer. Burying it in your work history is a missed opportunity.

Common mistakes to avoid

Including a photo on an Australian resume

Photos are not customary on Australian resumes. The Australian Human Rights Commission provides guidance against including personal details that could expose employers to discrimination claims. Save the headshot for your LinkedIn profile.

Using a US-style one-page format

Australian resumes are typically two to three pages. A one-page document reads as thin or under-prepared. Use the longer format to provide the comprehensive work history that Australian recruiters expect.

Listing personal details like age or marital status

Date of birth, marital status, religion, and tax file number have no place on an Australian resume. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact details and professional information.

Using American English spelling

"Organization" instead of "organisation", "analyze" instead of "analyse" — these signal you have copied a US template. Australian recruiters notice immediately. Use Australian English (which mostly follows British conventions) throughout.

Forgetting to mention visa status

If you are not an Australian citizen and require sponsorship, hiding this until a job offer wastes everyone's time. Mention your visa status briefly on your resume or in your cover letter. Australian employers appreciate the honesty.
