Canadian employers generally expect a resume (not a CV) that is concise, achievement-focused, and free of personal details like photos or marital status — closer in style to US resumes than UK or Gulf-region CVs. The Canadian Human Rights Act prohibits discrimination on several protected grounds, and including personal details puts employers in a difficult position.

This builder supports that format naturally: leave the optional photo field empty, focus your Work Experience section on measurable achievements, and keep the layout clean and scannable for Canadian hiring practices. The result reads as culturally fluent, not as a generic international document.

Canadian resumes are typically one to two pages. One page is preferred for graduates and early-career applicants; two pages is acceptable for experienced hires. The convention is similar to the US, with one important difference — Canadian resumes often include bilingual capability (English and French) and references to the official languages.

Achievement-focused writing is particularly valued in Canada. Canadian recruiters expect to see quantified outcomes — "increased sales by 23%", "managed a team of 12", "shipped product to 50,000 users". Avoid listing responsibilities without context. Numbers jump off the page and make your application memorable.

ATS usage is widespread in Canada, particularly among large employers, government departments, and the financial sector. Workday, Taleo, and Greenhouse are all commonly used. Switch on ATS Mode when applying through online portals — the single-column layout with standard section headings will parse reliably across all of these platforms.

Canadian English spelling follows British conventions for many words ("colour", "centre", "organise") but American conventions for others ("analyze", "tire"). When in doubt, mirror the spelling used in the job posting. Consistency within your document matters more than which convention you choose.

Right to work in Canada is a critical consideration. Canadian citizens and permanent residents do not need to state their status. If you are on a work permit, mention it briefly if you are comfortable doing so — Canadian employers are generally familiar with the work permit process and may be willing to sponsor permanent residency for the right candidate.

What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area, and one quantified achievement. Avoid objective statements — Canadian recruiters expect a summary that sells what you bring, not what you want.

Work experience in reverse chronological order

Most recent role first. Each entry should include job title, employer, dates (month and year), location, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Quantify outcomes wherever possible — Canadian recruiters expect measurable results.

Education and certifications

Degree, institution, and graduation year. Canadian recruiters also value professional certifications (PMP, CPA, CFA) and provincial designations (P.Eng for engineers, CPA for accountants). List these in a Certifications section if you have several.

Skills section with technical and soft skills

Group skills by category. Mirror the terminology used in Canadian job postings. If the role requires bilingual capability (English/French), state your proficiency levels explicitly in a Languages section.

Languages (if bilingual)

English and French are Canada's official languages. Bilingual capability is a significant advantage, particularly for federal government roles and roles in Quebec. State your proficiency level for each language (fluent, professional, conversational).

Volunteer experience (optional)

Canadian employers value community engagement. If you have significant volunteer experience, include it in a dedicated section. Treat each role like a work experience entry — describe what you did and the impact.

Tips for a stronger resume

Keep it to one or two pages

One page for graduates and early-career applicants. Two pages for experienced hires. Three pages is too many unless you are in academia or research. Canadian recruiters will not read past the second page.

Quantify your achievements

Canadian recruiters expect measurable results. "Increased sales by 23%", "managed a team of 12", "shipped product to 50,000 users". Numbers make achievements concrete and comparable across candidates.

Mirror Canadian English conventions

Canadian English blends British and American spelling — "colour" and "centre" (British) but "analyze" and "tire" (American). When in doubt, mirror the spelling used in the job posting. Consistency within your document matters more than which convention you choose.

Highlight bilingual capability

English/French bilingualism is a significant advantage in Canada, particularly for federal government roles and roles in Quebec, New Brunswick, and parts of Ontario. State your proficiency level for each language explicitly.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

Workday, Taleo, and Greenhouse are widely used in Canada. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct emails to recruiters or networking events.

Include volunteer experience

Canadian employers value community engagement. If you have significant volunteer experience, include it in a dedicated section. Treat each role like a work experience entry — describe what you did and the impact.

Adapt for the province you are applying to

Quebec has different conventions (CVs may be in French, photos are sometimes included in creative fields). Alberta and Ontario follow standard North American conventions. BC is more casual. Tailor your application for the specific province and sector you are targeting.

Common mistakes to avoid

Including a photo on a Canadian resume

Photos are generally not included on Canadian resumes. The Canadian Human Rights Act prohibits discrimination on several protected grounds, and including a photo puts employers in a difficult position. Save the headshot for your LinkedIn profile.

Listing personal details like age or marital status

Date of birth, marital status, religion, and social insurance number have no place on a Canadian resume. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact details and professional information.

Going over two pages

Canadian recruiters expect one to two pages. Three pages signals poor editing and disrespect for the recruiter's time. Be ruthless about cutting outdated roles, irrelevant coursework, and unrelated hobbies.

Using objective statements

"Seeking a challenging role in a dynamic organisation" tells the recruiter nothing. Modern Canadian resumes use a professional summary that positions you for the role, not an objective statement about what you want. Make the shift.

Forgetting to mention Canadian work eligibility

If you are a Canadian citizen or permanent resident, you do not need to state it. If you are on a work permit, mention it briefly if you are comfortable — Canadian employers are generally familiar with the process and may be willing to sponsor permanent residency for the right candidate.
