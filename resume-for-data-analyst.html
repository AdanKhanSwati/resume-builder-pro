What to include

A targeted technical summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your primary tools (SQL, Python, Tableau), years of experience, industry focus, and one quantified achievement. "Data analyst with 4 years experience in e-commerce, specialising in customer behaviour analysis using SQL, Python, and Tableau, driving $2M in incremental revenue through targeted recommendations".

Work experience with quantified business impact

Every bullet should connect data work to a business outcome. "Built churn prediction model identifying $2M in at-risk revenue", "automated weekly reporting pipeline saving 12 hours/week", "analysed A/B test results informing product changes that lifted conversion by 18%". Without business impact, your bullets are just technical tasks.

Technical skills grouped by category

SQL (PostgreSQL, MySQL, Snowflake, BigQuery), Python (pandas, NumPy, scikit-learn, matplotlib), R, Excel (advanced), BI tools (Tableau, Power BI, Looker, Mode), cloud platforms (AWS, GCP, Azure). Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match.

Projects with datasets and outcomes

Treat significant analyses as project entries. List the project name, the dataset, the tools used, and the business outcome. "Customer segmentation analysis — used k-means clustering on 2M-customer dataset in Python, identifying 5 segments that informed Q3 marketing strategy and drove 15% lift in retention".

Education with relevant coursework

Degree, institution, and graduation year. For data analysts, degrees in statistics, mathematics, computer science, economics, or related quantitative fields are valued. Add relevant coursework (machine learning, statistical inference, econometrics) for recent graduates. Add certifications (Google Data Analytics, IBM Data Science, AWS Data Analytics) if relevant.

Communication and stakeholder management

Data analysts must translate technical findings for non-technical stakeholders. Mention specific examples: "presented monthly insights to executive team", "trained sales team on dashboard usage", "collaborated with product managers to define success metrics". Communication is as important as technical skill.

Tips for a stronger resume

Connect every analysis to a business outcome

"Built churn prediction model" is a technical task. "Built churn prediction model identifying $2M in at-risk revenue, enabling retention campaigns that saved $800K" is a business outcome. Hiring managers care about revenue, cost, and decisions influenced — not the technical sophistication of your models.

Be specific about tools and versions

List the exact tools you have used — PostgreSQL, Snowflake, BigQuery, pandas, scikit-learn, Tableau, Power BI, Looker. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description. Generic claims like "experienced with data analysis tools" tell the recruiter nothing.

Showcase projects with measurable impact

Treat significant analyses as project entries. List the project name, dataset, tools, and outcome. Three concrete projects with measurable impact beat ten generic bullets. Include GitHub or portfolio links where possible — proof beats claims.

Switch on ATS Mode for online applications

Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, and iCIMS are widely used in data analyst recruitment. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct recruiter emails or networking events.

Mention stakeholder communication

Data analysts must translate technical findings for non-technical stakeholders. Mention specific examples: "presented monthly insights to executive team", "trained sales team on dashboard usage". Communication is as important as technical skill — do not assume the recruiter knows this.

Include a GitHub or portfolio link

If you have public analyses, dashboards, or code on GitHub, link to it. Recruiters and hiring managers will click through. Make sure your repositories have READMEs and clean code. A well-documented Jupyter notebook demonstrating a real analysis beats any list of skills.

Tailor for the role type

Product analytics roles want experimentation and user behaviour. Marketing analytics roles want attribution and campaign measurement. Finance analytics roles want forecasting and variance analysis. Operations analytics roles want process optimisation. Tailor your summary and bullets for each application.

Common mistakes to avoid

Listing technical tasks without business impact

"Wrote SQL queries" tells the recruiter nothing. "Built automated reporting pipeline in SQL and Python, saving 12 hours/week and reducing reporting errors by 90%" tells them exactly what you achieved. Connect every analysis to a business outcome.

Vague tool lists

"Experienced with SQL, Python, and BI tools" tells the recruiter nothing. List specific tools — PostgreSQL, Snowflake, pandas, scikit-learn, Tableau, Power BI. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description. Generic claims signal shallow expertise.

Overemphasising academic projects

If you have professional data analyst experience, lead with that. Academic projects (coursework, dissertations) should be a small section at the bottom of your document. Three professional projects with business impact beat ten academic projects without context.

Going over two pages

Data analyst resumes should be one to two pages. One page for junior analysts; two pages for experienced hires with multiple projects and tools to detail. Three pages is too many for most data analyst roles — recruiters will not read past the second page.

Omitting stakeholder communication

Data analysts must communicate findings to non-technical stakeholders. If you only list technical skills and projects, you look like someone who cannot translate analysis into action. Mention specific examples of presentations, training, or collaboration with business teams.
