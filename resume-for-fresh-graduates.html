What to include

Education at the top

For graduates within three years of finishing their degree, Education should sit above Work Experience. Include degree, institution, graduation year, GPA (if 3.5+), relevant coursework, and any academic honours or dean's list mentions.

Academic and capstone projects

Treat each significant project like a work experience entry. List the project name, your role, the tools used, and the outcome. "Built a machine learning model that classified customer support tickets with 87% accuracy" beats "Did a final-year project on machine learning".

Internships and part-time work

Even unrelated part-time work demonstrates reliability, time management, and customer service skills. Frame each role around transferable skills: "Managed a team of four shift workers" shows leadership even if the job was at a coffee shop.

Technical and soft skills

List programming languages, software, tools, and methodologies you have used in coursework or projects. Add soft skills (communication, teamwork, problem-solving) sparingly — show them through your project descriptions instead of just listing them.

Extracurriculars and leadership

Society leadership, sports team captaincy, volunteer coordination, or organising a hackathon all demonstrate initiative and soft skills. Include one or two of the most relevant — do not list every club you ever attended.

Certifications and online courses

AWS, Google, Coursera, or edX certifications show self-directed learning beyond your degree. List them in a Certifications section with the issuing organisation and completion date. Drop anything older than three years unless particularly relevant.

Tips for a stronger resume

Lead with your strongest asset

If your degree is from a top university, lead with Education. If your projects are impressive, lead with Projects. If you had a great internship, lead with that. The first section the recruiter sees should be your strongest selling point.

Quantify your academic work

"Top 5% of cohort", "Achieved 94% on capstone project", "Selected from 80 applicants for funded research position". Numbers make academic achievements concrete and comparable across candidates.

Mirror the language of graduate programmes

Graduate schemes often use specific competency frameworks ("leadership", "analytical thinking", "commercial awareness"). Use this language in your summary and bullet points so both ATS software and human reviewers recognise the match.

Keep it to one page

Fresh graduate documents should never exceed one page. You do not have enough experience to justify two. Be ruthless — cut the irrelevant coursework, the unrelated hobbies, and the high school achievements. Quality over quantity.

Include a link to your GitHub or portfolio

If you are applying for a technical or creative role, link to live examples of your work. Three concrete projects on GitHub are more compelling than a long list of skills on paper. Make sure the repositories have README files and clean code.

Get feedback from your university careers service

Most universities offer free CV reviews. Use them. A careers advisor will catch generic phrasing, missing sections, and formatting issues you have gone blind to after staring at the document for hours.

Apply early in the graduate recruitment cycle

Most graduate schemes open applications in September and close by December for roles starting the following summer. Applying in the first two weeks of the window meaningfully improves your chances — recruiters review applications as they arrive.

Common mistakes to avoid

Listing every course you ever took

Include only courses relevant to the role you are applying for. A recruiter does not need to see your first-year electives. Five well-chosen courses beat a comprehensive list of twenty.

Including high school achievements

Once you have a university degree, high school is irrelevant — unless you are applying for a role that specifically requires it (some government positions, for example). Drop the high school section entirely.

Using "Entry-level" as your job title

If you have no work experience, do not invent a job title. Use the section for projects, internships, or volunteer work instead. Fake job titles are obvious and damage your credibility.

Describing projects in passive, vague language

"Worked on a team project to build a website" tells the recruiter nothing. "Led a four-person team to ship a React-based e-commerce demo with a 95 Lighthouse score" tells them exactly what you did and how. Active, specific language wins.

Sending the same document to every employer

Graduate schemes vary enormously in what they look for. A consulting firm wants evidence of analytical thinking; a tech company wants technical depth; a charity wants commitment to a cause. Tailor your summary and project order for each application.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I make a good document with no work experience?

Yes — focus on education, academic projects, internships, and relevant coursework. The builder is structured to highlight these sections clearly. Many successful graduate applications have no formal work experience section at all.

Is this builder free for students and graduates?

Yes, it is completely free with no signup required. There is no student discount to claim because there is no paid tier in the first place. Build, edit, and export as many versions as you need at no cost.

Will my document pass ATS screening?

Yes, switch on ATS Mode for a clean, scannable format that is friendly to applicant tracking systems used by graduate schemes. The single-column layout with standard section headings parses reliably across every common ATS platform.

Can I include university projects?

Yes, the Projects section is designed for exactly this — list the project name, your role, the tools used, and the outcome. Treat each significant project like a work experience entry.

How long should a fresh graduate's document be?

Usually one page is enough — focus on quality and relevance over length. Two pages is acceptable only if you have extensive project work, publications, or relevant experience to detail. Three pages is too many at this stage.

Should I include my GPA?

Include your GPA if it is 3.5 or above (or the equivalent in your grading system). Below that, leave it off — recruiters will assume the worst if the number is missing, but they will know the worst if it is there. Many graduate schemes ask for transcripts separately anyway.

Can I include high school achievements?

Once you have a university degree, high school is generally irrelevant. Drop it entirely unless you are applying for a role that specifically requires it. The space is better used for university projects and internships.

What if I changed degrees or universities?

List both institutions if the change reflects positively on you (transferring to a more prestigious university, switching to a more relevant field). Otherwise, list only your final degree. Long explanations of academic history are rarely read.
