What to include

A targeted design summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, design specialism (branding, digital, print, motion, UX/UI), and one quantified achievement. "Graphic designer with 5 years experience in brand identity and digital design, specialising in fashion and lifestyle brands, with work featured in 3 industry publications".

Work experience with quantified client outcomes

Every bullet should connect design work to a business outcome. "Redesigned e-commerce site lifting conversion by 23%", "developed brand identity adopted across 40+ marketing assets", "created social media graphics driving 4x engagement rate". Without outcomes, your bullets are just task lists.

Design software and tools

List the specific tools you have used — Adobe Creative Suite (Photoshop, Illustrator, InDesign, XD, After Effects), Figma, Sketch, Canva, Procreate. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description. For UX/UI roles, add wireframing and prototyping tools.

Projects with portfolio links

Treat significant design projects as project entries. List the project name, your role, the client, the deliverables, and the outcome. Include portfolio links where possible — proof beats claims. Three concrete projects with measurable outcomes beat ten generic bullets.

Portfolio link at the top

Include a link to your online portfolio (Behance, Dribbble, personal website) in your contact details at the top of your document. Recruiters will click through. Make sure your portfolio is polished, current, and demonstrates range — branding, digital, print, motion if relevant.

Education and design qualifications

Degree, institution, and graduation year. For graphic designers, degrees in graphic design, visual communication, or related fields are valued. Add relevant coursework or specialisations for recent graduates. Mention design awards or recognitions if you have them.

Tips for a stronger resume

Quantify design outcomes

Every bullet should connect design work to a business outcome. "Redesigned e-commerce site lifting conversion by 23%", "developed brand identity adopted across 40+ marketing assets", "created social media graphics driving 4x engagement rate". Without outcomes, your bullets are just task lists.

Include a portfolio link at the top

Portfolio links are non-negotiable for graphic designers. Include a link to your online portfolio (Behance, Dribbble, personal website) in your contact details at the top of your document. Recruiters will click through. Make sure your portfolio is polished and current.

Be specific about design software

List the exact tools you have used — Adobe Creative Suite (Photoshop, Illustrator, InDesign, XD, After Effects), Figma, Sketch, Canva, Procreate. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description. Generic claims like "proficient with design software" tell the recruiter nothing.

Switch on ATS Mode for online applications

Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, and iCIMS are widely used in design recruitment. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct recruiter emails — but keep the layout clean even in styled mode.

Tailor for the design discipline

Branding roles want identity systems and guidelines. Digital design roles want web and app interfaces. Print roles want layout and typography. Motion roles want After Effects and Premiere. UX/UI roles want wireframing and prototyping. Tailor your summary, skills, and portfolio selection for each application.

Showcase range in your portfolio

Recruiters want to see range — branding, digital, print, motion if relevant. Curate your portfolio to demonstrate versatility, but lead with the work most relevant to the role you are applying for. A focused portfolio beats a sprawling one.

Include client names if permissible

If you can disclose client names (particularly recognisable brands), mention them. "Designed brand identity for [well-known fashion retailer]" carries more weight than "designed brand identity for retail client". If NDA prevents disclosure, mention the industry and scale instead — "global fashion retailer with $500M annual revenue".

Common mistakes to avoid

Over-designing the resume itself

A heavily designed resume with custom layouts, infographics, and decorative elements often breaks ATS parsing. Keep your resume clean and let your portfolio do the visual heavy lifting. The resume should be a clear, scannable document — not a design showcase.

Listing design tasks without outcomes

"Created social media graphics" tells the recruiter nothing. "Created social media graphics driving 4x engagement rate and 25% follower growth over 6 months" tells them exactly what you achieved. Connect design work to business outcomes wherever possible.

Omitting a portfolio link

Portfolio links are non-negotiable for graphic designers. Without one, recruiters cannot verify your design capability. Include a link to your online portfolio (Behance, Dribbble, personal website) in your contact details at the top of your document.

Going over two pages

Graphic designer resumes should be one to two pages. One page for junior designers; two pages for experienced hires with multiple projects to detail. Three pages is too many for most design roles — recruiters will not read past the second page.

Using generic design buzzwords

"Creative", "innovative", "detail-oriented", "passionate designer" — every applicant uses these. Show these qualities through your portfolio and quantified outcomes instead. "Redesigned e-commerce site lifting conversion by 23%" demonstrates creativity and commercial awareness better than any adjective.
