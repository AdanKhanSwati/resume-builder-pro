Indian resumes are typically one to two pages. One page is preferred for freshers (recent graduates); two pages is standard for experienced hires. Three pages is acceptable only for very senior roles or academic positions. Indian recruiters will not read past the second page for most corporate roles.

Personal details are commonly included on Indian resumes — date of birth, languages known, permanent address, and sometimes father's name (particularly for traditional employers). Modern multinational employers (particularly in IT and consulting) prefer these omitted. Mirror the conventions of the specific employer you are targeting.

What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area, and one quantified achievement. Indian recruiters often use the summary to filter by experience level and domain.

Work experience in reverse chronological order

Most recent role first. Each entry should include job title, employer, dates (month and year), location, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Quantify outcomes wherever possible — Indian recruiters expect measurable results, particularly in IT and consulting.

Education with academic details

Degree, institution, graduation year, and percentage or CGPA. Indian recruiters often expect academic details (10th, 12th, degree) for freshers. Experienced hires can compress this to a single section. Add professional certifications (CA, CS, CFA, PMP) — these are highly valued in India.

Comprehensive skills section

Technical skills (programming languages, frameworks, tools, databases), soft skills, and domain expertise. Group by category. Mirror the terminology of the job description. Skills sections are particularly important in India, especially for IT and engineering roles.

Languages with proficiency

English and Hindi at minimum, with regional languages (Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, Marathi, Kannada, Malayalam, Gujarati, Punjabi, etc.) valued in their respective states. List your languages with proficiency levels (read, write, speak).

Personal details (commonly included)

Date of birth, languages known, permanent address, and sometimes father's name. Modern multinational employers prefer these omitted. Mirror the conventions of the specific employer you are targeting — traditional Indian companies expect them; multinationals do not.

Tips for a stronger resume

Use Indian English conventions

Indian English mostly follows British spelling ("organisation", "specialise", "colour") but has unique vocabulary ("CTC", "fresher", "releaving letter"). Mirror the language of the job posting. Consistency matters more than which convention you choose.

Keep it to one or two pages

One page for freshers (recent graduates). Two pages for experienced hires. Three pages is acceptable only for very senior roles or academic positions. Indian recruiters will not read past the second page for most corporate roles.

Quantify your achievements

Indian recruiters expect measurable results, particularly in IT and consulting. "Increased sales by 23%", "managed a team of 12", "shipped product to 50,000 users". Numbers make achievements concrete and comparable across candidates.

Tailor your skills section for IT roles

For IT and engineering roles, list technical skills comprehensively — programming languages, frameworks, tools, databases, cloud platforms. Group by category. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

Workday, Taleo, Greenhouse, Naukri's ResDex, and iCIMS are widely used in India. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct emails to recruiters or networking events.

Apply through Naukri, LinkedIn, and direct channels

Naukri is India's largest job portal. Register and apply through Naukri, but also apply through LinkedIn and directly through company careers pages. Multiple channels increase your chances of being noticed by recruiters.

Mention notice period and current CTC

Indian recruiters often ask about notice period and current CTC (cost to company) early in the process. Have this information ready. Mention your notice period in your cover letter or email if it is shorter than the standard 60-90 days — it can be a competitive advantage.

Common mistakes to avoid

Including irrelevant personal details for multinational employers

Date of birth, father's name, marital status, and religion are commonly included on traditional Indian CVs but are not expected by multinational employers. Mirror the conventions of the specific employer you are targeting — when in doubt, omit.

Going over two pages without cause

Indian recruiters expect one to two pages. Three pages is acceptable only for very senior roles or academic positions. Be ruthless about cutting outdated roles, irrelevant coursework, and unrelated hobbies. Indian recruiters will not read past the second page.

Using a generic skills list

For IT and engineering roles, list technical skills comprehensively and group by category. A generic list of "Java, Python, SQL" tells the recruiter nothing about your depth. Specify frameworks (Spring, Django), tools (Git, Docker, Kubernetes), and domains (fintech, e-commerce, healthcare).

Spelling mistakes and grammatical errors

Indian recruiters are notoriously strict about spelling and grammar. A single typo can drop your application to the bottom of the pile. Run a spellcheck, read it out loud once, and ask a friend to scan it. The free builder includes a live preview so mistakes are easier to spot.

Sending the same CV to every employer

Indian employers vary enormously in what they look for. An IT services company wants technology stack experience; a consulting firm wants analytical thinking; a startup wants initiative and adaptability. Tailor your summary, skills, and section order for each application — five tailored versions beat fifty generic ones.
