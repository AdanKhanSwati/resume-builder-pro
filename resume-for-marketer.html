Marketing resumes live and die by quantified outcomes. Hiring managers want to see campaigns you have run, channels you have grown, leads you have generated, and revenue you have influenced — not a list of marketing buzzwords. A marketer who cannot quantify their impact looks like a marketer who has not had any.

Marketing recruiters look for specific channel expertise: SEO, SEM, paid social (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok), email marketing, content marketing, influencer marketing, affiliate marketing, PR, events. List the specific channels you have managed, with quantified outcomes for each. "Grew organic Instagram following from 5K to 80K in 18 months, generating 12% of total e-commerce revenue" beats "managed social media accounts".

Tools and platforms matter for marketers. List the specific tools you have used — Google Analytics, Google Ads, Facebook Ads Manager, HubSpot, Marketo, Mailchimp, Klaviyo, SEMrush, Ahrefs, Hootsuite, Buffer. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match.

What to include

A targeted marketing summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest channel (SEO, paid social, content, email, etc.), and one quantified achievement. "Growth marketer with 5 years scaling B2B SaaS companies, specialising in paid acquisition and lifecycle email, driving 3x ARR growth at last role".

Work experience with quantified outcomes

Every bullet should have a number. "Grew organic search traffic by 240% over 18 months", "managed $500K annual paid social budget with 4x ROAS", "built email programme generating 28% of total revenue". Marketing recruiters expect measurable results — without numbers, your bullets are just responsibility lists.

Channel expertise clearly listed

List the specific channels you have managed: SEO, SEM, paid social (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok), email marketing, content marketing, influencer marketing, affiliate marketing, PR, events. Quantify outcomes for each channel you mention.

Tools and platforms

List the specific tools you have used — Google Analytics, Google Ads, Facebook Ads Manager, HubSpot, Marketo, Mailchimp, Klaviyo, SEMrush, Ahrefs, Hootsuite, Buffer. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match.

Campaign highlights in a Projects section

Treat significant campaigns as project entries. List the campaign name, your role, the channels used, the budget, and the outcome. "Q4 holiday campaign — managed $200K multi-channel budget across paid social and email, driving $1.2M in incremental revenue at 6x ROAS".

Certifications (if relevant)

Google Ads certifications, Google Analytics certifications, HubSpot certifications, Facebook Blueprint certifications, and content marketing certifications can be valuable differentiators. List them in a Certifications section with the issuing body and date. Drop certifications older than three years unless still current.

Tips for a stronger resume

Quantify every marketing bullet

Every bullet should have a number. "Grew organic search traffic by 240%", "managed $500K annual paid social budget with 4x ROAS", "built email programme generating 28% of total revenue". Marketing recruiters expect measurable results — without numbers, your bullets are just responsibility lists.

Be specific about channels and platforms

List the exact channels (SEO, SEM, paid social, email, content) and platforms (Google Ads, Facebook Ads Manager, HubSpot, Marketo, Klaviyo) you have used. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match. Generic claims like "experienced with digital marketing" tell the recruiter nothing.

Use marketing metrics correctly

Use the right metrics for each channel: ROAS for paid, open rate and CTR for email, organic traffic and keyword rankings for SEO, engagement rate and follower growth for social. Mixing metrics across channels signals a lack of depth. Be precise.

Switch on ATS Mode for online applications

Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, and iCIMS are widely used in marketing recruitment. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct recruiter emails or networking events.

Highlight cross-channel campaigns

If you have run cross-channel campaigns (combining SEO, paid, email, social), mention it explicitly. Cross-channel expertise is highly valued because it demonstrates strategic thinking and the ability to coordinate multiple workstreams. Quantify the combined outcome.

Tailor for the role type

Performance marketing roles want paid acquisition and ROAS metrics. Brand marketing roles want awareness and consideration metrics. Content marketing roles want traffic and engagement metrics. Lifecycle marketing roles want retention and LTV metrics. Tailor your summary and bullets for each application.

Include a portfolio link if you have one

If you have a personal website, case study portfolio, or public marketing work (blog posts, campaigns, talks), link to it. Recruiters and hiring managers will click through. Make sure the portfolio is polished — an empty or dormant portfolio is worse than no link at all.

Common mistakes to avoid

Using vague marketing buzzwords

"Passionate marketer", "results-driven", "strategic thinker", "creative storyteller" — every applicant uses these. Show these qualities through quantified outcomes instead. "Grew organic search traffic by 240% over 18 months" demonstrates strategic thinking better than any adjective.

Listing responsibilities instead of achievements

"Managed paid social campaigns" tells the recruiter nothing. "Managed $500K annual paid social budget across Facebook and Instagram, achieving 4x ROAS and driving 35% of total e-commerce revenue" tells them exactly what you achieved. Quantify outcomes wherever possible.

Omitting tools and platforms

List the specific tools you have used — Google Analytics, Google Ads, Facebook Ads Manager, HubSpot, Marketo, Mailchimp, Klaviyo, SEMrush, Ahrefs. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description. Omitting tools forces the recruiter to guess — and they will usually guess wrong.

Going over two pages

Marketing resumes should be one to two pages. One page for junior marketers; two pages for experienced hires with multiple campaigns and channels to detail. Three pages is too many for most marketing roles — recruiters will not read past the second page.

Using vanity metrics

"Got 10K likes on a post" is a vanity metric. "Generated 500 qualified leads from a single LinkedIn campaign at $32 cost per lead, converting to $180K in pipeline" is a business metric. Hiring managers care about revenue, pipeline, and qualified leads — not likes, impressions, or follower counts unless they translate to business outcomes.
