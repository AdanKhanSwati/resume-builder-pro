What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area (executive support, office management, data entry, etc.), and one quantified achievement. "Office manager with 6 years of experience streamlining operations for fast-growing startups, specialising in vendor management and process automation".

Work experience with quantified outcomes

Each role should include title, employer, dates, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Quantify outcomes wherever possible — meetings scheduled, costs reduced, processes streamlined, time saved. Numbers make administrative work concrete and comparable across candidates.

Software and tools proficiency

List the specific tools you have used — Microsoft Office (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook), Google Workspace, accounting tools (QuickBooks, Xero), CRM systems (Salesforce, HubSpot), project management tools (Asana, Trello, Monday.com). Mirror the exact terminology of the job description.

Education and certifications

Degree, institution, and graduation year. For office roles, professional certifications (CAP — Certified Administrative Professional, Microsoft Office Specialist, Google Workspace certifications) can be valuable differentiators. List them in a Certifications section.

Languages (if relevant)

If the role requires multilingual support, list your languages with proficiency levels. Multilingual capability is valuable in office roles at international companies, customer-facing roles, or roles supporting global teams.

Soft skills demonstrated through achievements

Avoid listing generic soft skills ("organised", "detail-oriented", "team player"). Instead, demonstrate them through your achievement bullets. "Coordinated logistics for 50-person offsite event, managing vendor contracts and travel arrangements with zero errors" shows organisation and attention to detail better than any adjective.

Tips for a stronger resume

Quantify your administrative impact

Every bullet should have a metric. "Managed calendar for executive team of 6, scheduling 200+ meetings monthly", "reduced office supply costs by 18% through vendor renegotiation", "streamlined onboarding process, reducing new hire setup time from 5 days to 2 days". Numbers make administrative work concrete.

Be specific about software proficiency

List the exact tools you have used — Microsoft Office, Google Workspace, QuickBooks, Salesforce, Asana, Slack. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match. Generic claims like "proficient with computers" tell the recruiter nothing.

Highlight reliability and discretion

Office roles often involve handling confidential information, managing executive schedules, or coordinating sensitive meetings. Mention these responsibilities explicitly if relevant. "Managed confidential HR documents for 200+ employees with zero breaches" demonstrates discretion better than any adjective.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

Workday, Taleo, iCIMS, and Indeed's platform are widely used for office and administrative roles. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct recruiter emails or networking events.

Tailor for the specific role type

Executive assistant roles want calendar management and discretion. Office manager roles want vendor management and process improvement. Receptionist roles want customer service and presentation. Data entry roles want speed and accuracy. Tailor your summary, skills, and section order for each application.

Mention process improvements

Administrative professionals who can identify and implement process improvements are highly valued. If you have automated a manual process, reduced costs, or streamlined workflows, mention it explicitly with measurable outcomes. This separates you from administrators who only execute existing processes.

Include event coordination experience

If you have coordinated events (meetings, offsites, conferences), mention it explicitly. Event coordination demonstrates project management, vendor negotiation, and attention to detail — all valued in office roles. Quantify the scope — "coordinated annual sales kick-off for 200 attendees, managing $50K budget and 12 vendors".

Common mistakes to avoid

Listing responsibilities instead of achievements

"Managed executive calendar" tells the recruiter nothing. "Managed calendar for executive team of 6, scheduling 200+ meetings monthly with 99% conflict-free rate, reducing scheduling-related escalations by 60%" tells them exactly what you achieved. Quantify outcomes wherever possible.

Generic software proficiency claims

"Proficient with Microsoft Office" tells the recruiter nothing. List the specific tools — Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook — and your level of expertise with each. Mention specific examples of how you have used these tools to deliver outcomes.

Including irrelevant personal details

Date of birth, marital status, religion, and national ID numbers have no place on a modern office resume. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact details and professional information.

Going over two pages

Office resumes should be one to two pages. One page for entry-level administrative roles; two pages for experienced hires. Three pages is too many for most office roles — recruiters will not read past the second page.

Listing generic soft skills

"Organised", "detail-oriented", "team player" — every applicant claims these. Demonstrate them through your achievement bullets instead. "Coordinated logistics for 50-person offsite event, managing vendor contracts and travel arrangements with zero errors" shows organisation and attention to detail better than any adjective.
