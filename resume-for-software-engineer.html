A strong software engineering resume needs to do two things well: clearly show your technical stack and tools, and demonstrate measurable impact — performance improvements, scale, or features shipped — rather than just listing responsibilities. Tech recruiters and hiring managers scan for specific keywords and quantified outcomes, not generic job descriptions.

This builder is structured to support exactly that, with a dedicated Skills section for technical and soft skills, a Projects section for showcasing live work, GitHub links, and impact metrics, and AI-assisted bullet point tools to help you turn your work into results-driven statements. The result reads as a credible engineering CV, not a generic corporate document.

Build a clean, technically-focused document that passes ATS screening at tech companies and clearly communicates your engineering experience to human reviewers — free, with no signup required. The same data can produce a styled version for direct recruiter emails and an ATS-friendly version for online applications.

Tech recruiting is dominated by ATS platforms — Greenhouse, Lever, Workday, Ashby, and Gem are all widely used. These systems parse your document, extract your skills and experience, and match them against the job description. A multi-column layout, an unusual font, or text inside a graphic can all cause your information to be misparsed or dropped entirely.

Software engineering resumes should be one page for applicants with under ten years of experience, and at most two pages for senior hires. Recruiters will not read past the second page. Be ruthless about cutting older roles, irrelevant coursework, and bullet points that do not demonstrate impact. Quality beats quantity every time.

The skills section is particularly important for software engineers. Group your skills by category: languages (Python, Java, Go, JavaScript), frameworks (React, Spring, Django), tools (Git, Docker, Kubernetes), databases (PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Redis), and cloud platforms (AWS, GCP, Azure). Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match.

The Projects section is your secret weapon. Three concrete projects with measurable outcomes — "shipped a React-based dashboard used by 200 engineers daily", "reduced API latency by 40% through query optimisation", "built a Python ETL pipeline processing 50M events/day" — beat ten generic bullet points about responsibilities. Include GitHub links so recruiters can verify the work.

What to include

A targeted technical summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your primary stack, years of experience, and one quantified achievement. "Backend engineer with 6 years building Python microservices on AWS, specialising in high-throughput APIs serving 10M+ requests/day" is far stronger than "passionate software engineer".

Work experience with engineering impact

Each role should include title, employer, dates, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Every bullet should have a metric — latency reduction, throughput improvement, user count, deployment frequency. Engineering recruiters expect measurable outcomes, not responsibility lists.

A comprehensive, well-grouped skills section

Group by category: languages, frameworks, tools, databases, cloud platforms. Mirror the exact terminology of the job description so ATS systems recognise the match. Only include skills you can defend in a technical interview — inflated skill lists get caught quickly.

Projects with GitHub links and impact metrics

Three concrete projects with measurable outcomes. Include GitHub repository links so recruiters can verify the work. Treat each project like a work experience entry: project name, your role, the tech used, and the outcome. Open-source contributions count here too.

Education and certifications

Degree, institution, and graduation year. For software engineers, certifications (AWS, GCP, Azure, Kubernetes) are valuable if relevant to the role. Drop certifications older than five years unless particularly prestigious. Self-taught bootcamp graduates should list their bootcamp here.

Open-source contributions and community involvement

If you contribute to open-source projects, mention it. If you have a Stack Overflow reputation above 10K, mention it. If you speak at conferences or write technical blog posts, mention it. These signals distinguish you from applicants who only code at work.

Tips for a stronger resume

Quantify engineering impact

Every bullet should have a metric. "Reduced API latency by 40%", "shipped feature to 200K users", "cut deployment time from 30 minutes to 90 seconds", "processed 50M events/day". Engineering recruiters expect measurable outcomes — without numbers, your bullets are just responsibility lists.

Mirror the exact tech stack from the job description

If the posting asks for "Python, Django, PostgreSQL, AWS", list those exact terms in your skills section. ATS systems match on terminology. If you used a similar but different stack (Flask instead of Django), mention both — but be honest about your depth in each.

Switch on ATS Mode for online applications

Greenhouse, Lever, Workday, Ashby, and Gem all parse your document programmatically. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct recruiter emails or networking events.

Include GitHub and portfolio links

Add your GitHub profile URL and a personal portfolio link if you have one. Recruiters and hiring managers will click through. Make sure your GitHub profile is polished — pinned repositories with READMEs, recent commits, and clean code. An empty or dormant GitHub profile is worse than no link at all.

Use technical vocabulary correctly

Software engineers review your document as much as recruiters do. Use terms correctly — do not say "implemented microservices" if you only deployed a monolith. Engineering reviewers will catch inaccuracies in technical interviews and your credibility will suffer.

Keep it to one page for under ten years of experience

Two pages is acceptable for senior hires. Three pages is too many for any engineering role. Be ruthless about cutting older roles, irrelevant coursework, and bullets that do not demonstrate impact. Quality beats quantity.

Run a final keyword check against the job posting

After building, scan your document for the ten most important terms from the job description. If any are missing, work them in naturally. The builder makes this easy — just edit and re-export.

Common mistakes to avoid

Listing every technology you have ever touched

An exhaustive skills list signals you cannot distinguish between deep expertise and surface familiarity. Group skills by category and be honest about your depth. Five well-chosen skills beat thirty generic ones — engineering reviewers will probe the weakest item on your list.

Using vague, responsibility-focused bullets

"Worked on the backend" tells the recruiter nothing. "Built a Python microservice handling 10M requests/day with 99.9% uptime" tells them exactly what you did. Engineering recruiters expect measurable impact, not responsibility lists copied from a job spec.

Including unrelated personal projects

A weekend hackathon project from five years ago is not relevant. A recent open-source contribution to a well-known library is. Curate your projects section ruthlessly — three impressive projects beat ten mediocre ones. Recruiters will click through to GitHub, so make sure the code holds up.

Going over two pages

Engineering recruiters will not read past the second page. One page is preferred for under ten years of experience. Two pages is acceptable for senior hires. Three pages signals poor editing and disrespect for the recruiter's time.

Failing to mention impact and scale

"Built a web application" tells the recruiter nothing. "Built a React-based dashboard used by 200 engineers daily, reducing incident response time by 35%" tells them everything. Numbers make your engineering work concrete and comparable across candidates.
