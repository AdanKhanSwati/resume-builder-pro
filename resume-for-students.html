What to include

Education with relevant detail

Degree, institution, expected graduation year, and GPA (if 3.5+). Add relevant coursework, honours, and dean's list mentions. For students, Education is usually the strongest section — give it space at the top.

Projects, both academic and personal

Treat each significant project as a work experience entry. List the project name, your role, the tools used, and the outcome. Include GitHub or live links where possible — proof beats claims.

Internships and part-time work

Even unrelated work shows reliability and soft skills. Frame each role around transferable skills: "Managed opening shifts", "Trained new starters", "Resolved customer complaints". Numbers make the experience concrete.

Extracurricular leadership

Society committee roles, sports captaincy, volunteer coordination, or event organising all demonstrate initiative and soft skills. Include one or two of the most relevant — quality beats quantity.

Skills grouped by category

Technical skills (programming languages, software, tools), soft skills (communication, leadership, problem-solving), and languages (with proficiency level). Mirror the language of the role you are applying for.

Certifications and online courses

AWS, Google, Coursera, or edX certifications show self-directed learning. List them with the issuing organisation and completion date. Drop anything older than three years unless particularly relevant.

Tips for a stronger resume

Lead with your strongest section

Most students should lead with Education. If you have an impressive internship, lead with Work Experience. If you have stellar projects, lead with Projects. The first section the recruiter sees should be your best selling point.

Quantify your academic and extracurricular work

"Top 10% of cohort", "Selected from 80 applicants", "Raised £2,000 for charity", "Captain of university basketball team (24-3 season)". Numbers make your achievements concrete and comparable.

Use the AI assist to strengthen your bullet points

Most students write vague bullets like "worked on a team project". The AI tool can rewrite this as "Led a four-person team to ship a React-based web app with 95 Lighthouse score". Accept the suggestions that fit, reject those that do not.

Tailor for each application type

An internship application wants evidence of learning potential. A part-time job wants reliability and customer service. A scholarship wants academic excellence. Adjust your summary and section order for each — the same data, different framing.

Include links to your work

GitHub for code, Behance or Dribbble for design, a personal website for writing. Live links to actual work beat claims on paper. Make sure the repositories and portfolios are polished — recruiters will click through.

Keep it to one page

Student documents should never exceed one page. You do not have enough material to justify two. Be ruthless about cutting irrelevant coursework, unrelated hobbies, and outdated achievements.

Get a second opinion

Your university careers service, a tutor, or a friend in the year above can all catch issues you have gone blind to. A five-minute review by a fresh pair of eyes catches typos, awkward phrasing, and missing sections.

Common mistakes to avoid

Listing every course you have ever taken

Include only courses relevant to the role. A recruiter scanning your document does not need to see your first-year electives. Five well-chosen courses beat a comprehensive list of twenty.

Including high school information

Once you are at university, high school is generally irrelevant. Drop it entirely unless you are applying for a role that specifically asks for it. The space is better used for university-level work.

Describing projects vaguely

"Worked on a team project to build a website" tells the recruiter nothing. "Led a four-person team to ship a React-based e-commerce demo with a 95 Lighthouse score" tells them exactly what you did. Specificity wins.

Overusing buzzwords

"Passionate", "hardworking", "team player", "results-driven" — recruiters read these words dozens of times per document. Show these qualities through your achievements instead of claiming them. Strong verbs and quantified outcomes beat adjectives.

Sending the same document to every employer

An internship at a tech company wants different evidence than a part-time role at a cafe or a scholarship application. Tailor your summary, skills, and section order for each — five tailored applications beat fifty generic ones.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this builder suitable for students?

Yes, it is designed to work well for students with limited or no work experience, with sections for education, coursework, projects, and activities. You can disable the Work Experience section entirely if it does not apply.

Can I list volunteer work and extracurriculars?

Yes, there are dedicated sections for Volunteer Experience, and you can use Achievements or Interests for extracurriculars. Treat significant volunteer roles like work experience entries — describe what you did and the impact.

Is it free to use?

Yes, completely free with no signup required. There is no student discount to claim because there is no paid tier. Build, edit, and export as many versions as you need at no cost.

Can I update my document later for different applications?

Yes, your data is saved in your browser so you can keep editing and re-export anytime. To move between devices, save your data as a JSON file and re-import it on the other side.

Does it work for internship applications?

Yes, it is a great fit for internship, part-time, and entry-level applications. The Projects section is particularly useful for internships — recruiters want to see what you can build, not just what you have studied.

Should I include my GPA?

Include your GPA if it is 3.5 or above (or the equivalent in your grading system). Below that, leave it off. Recruiters will assume the worst if the number is missing, but they will know the worst if it is there.

Can I include high school achievements?

Once you are at university, high school is generally irrelevant. Drop it entirely unless the role specifically asks for it. The space is better used for university-level work, projects, and extracurriculars.

How long should a student document be?

One page is enough. You do not have enough material to justify two pages. Be ruthless about cutting irrelevant coursework, unrelated hobbies, and outdated achievements. Quality beats quantity.
