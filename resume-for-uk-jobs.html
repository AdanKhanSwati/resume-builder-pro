Applying for jobs in the United Kingdom often means presenting your CV in a slightly different style than other regions — typically without a photo, with a clear personal statement at the top, and a strong focus on work history and qualifications. UK recruiters expect a specific structure, and deviating from it can hurt your chances.

This builder lets you structure your CV accordingly: keep the optional photo off for a traditional UK-style format, write a concise professional summary (called a "personal statement" in the UK), and clearly lay out your experience and education in a format UK employers and recruitment software expect. The result reads as culturally fluent, not as a generic international document.

The UK CV convention is one to two pages — never more, regardless of seniority, unless you are in academia or research. Two pages is acceptable for experienced hires; one page is preferred for graduates and early-career applicants. Recruiters will not read past the second page, so do not waste it on outdated roles or irrelevant detail.

UK employers place particular weight on the personal statement. This is a three-to-four-line summary at the top of the document that positions you for the role. Avoid the cliché "hardworking team player with excellent communication skills" — recruiters have read that line a thousand times. Be specific: "Marketing executive with five years of B2B SaaS experience, specialising in content-led demand generation".

Photos are not included on UK CVs. Unlike CVs in some European and Gulf-region markets, the UK convention is to omit the photo to reduce the risk of discrimination claims during the screening process. The same applies to date of birth, marital status, and national insurance number — none of these belong on a modern UK CV.

ATS usage is widespread in the UK, particularly among large employers, recruitment agencies, and graduate schemes. Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, and iCIMS all have significant market share. Switch on ATS Mode when applying through any online portal — the single-column layout with standard section headings will parse reliably across all of these platforms.

Right to work in the UK is a critical consideration for employers. You do not need to state your nationality or visa status on your CV, but be prepared to discuss it at interview stage. If you require sponsorship, be honest about it — hiding it until a job offer wastes everyone's time and can damage your reputation in a small market.

What to include

A clear personal statement

Three to four lines at the top of the CV that position you for the role. Mention your current role, years of experience, specialism, and one quantified achievement. Avoid clichés — specificity wins in the UK market.

Work experience in reverse chronological order

Most recent role first. Each entry should include job title, employer, dates (month and year), and three to five achievement-focused bullets. UK recruiters expect this format — deviating from it raises red flags.

Education with relevant detail

Degree, institution, and graduation year. UK recruiters also expect A-levels (or equivalent) for graduates within five years of finishing their degree. Add GCSEs only if the role specifically requires them (some teaching and civil service roles).

Skills section with relevant competencies

Technical skills, languages, and any professional certifications. UK job postings often reference specific competencies — mirror the language so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match.

Right to work status (optional)

You are not required to state your visa status, but if you have indefinite leave to remain or British citizenship, mentioning it briefly can speed up the screening process. If you need sponsorship, save that conversation for the interview.

References line

"References available on request" is the UK convention. Do not include referee contact details directly on your CV — recruiters will ask for them later in the process. The line itself is increasingly considered optional, but it does no harm.

Tips for a stronger resume

Use British English spelling

"Organisation" not "organization", "specialise" not "specialize", "programme" not "program" (unless referring to computer software). Mixing British and American spelling signals carelessness to UK recruiters.

Keep it to two pages maximum

One page for graduates and early-career applicants. Two pages for experienced hires. Three pages is too many unless you are in academia, research, or have a substantial publications list. UK recruiters will not read past the second page.

Use British date formats

"March 2021 – Present" or "Mar 2021 – Present". Avoid the US format ("March 21, 2021") or the all-numeric format ("03/21") which can be confused with the US month/day format. Consistency is key — pick one format and stick with it.

Mirror the language of UK job postings

UK recruiters often reference specific frameworks (CIPD for HR, ACCA for finance, BCS for IT). Use the terminology from the posting so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match. Just ensure the usage is natural — keyword stuffing is detected.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

Workday, Greenhouse, and Lever are widely used in the UK. The single-column ATS layout parses reliably across all of these. Save the styled version for direct emails to recruiters or networking events.

Include a LinkedIn URL if your profile is polished

Most UK recruiters will look you up on LinkedIn before inviting you to interview. Make sure your profile is up to date, your photo is professional, and your headline matches the role you are applying for. Add the URL to your CV contact details.

Apply via the company's careers page when possible

Direct applications on the company's website usually go straight into their ATS. Email applications may be parsed separately or manually entered, which can introduce errors. When a posting offers both options, choose the website.

Common mistakes to avoid

Including a photo on a UK CV

Photos are not customary on UK CVs and can expose employers to discrimination claims. Most ATS platforms strip images anyway, which can corrupt your layout. Save the headshot for your LinkedIn profile.

Listing personal details like date of birth or marital status

These have no place on a modern UK CV. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. The Equality Act 2010 makes it illegal to discriminate on age, marital status, religion, and several other characteristics — do not put employers in a position where they have to consider these.

Going over two pages

UK recruiters expect one to two pages. Three pages signals poor editing and disrespect for the recruiter's time. Be ruthless about cutting outdated roles, irrelevant coursework, and unrelated hobbies.

Using American spelling or terminology

"Resume" instead of "CV", "organization" instead of "organisation", "college" instead of "university" — these signal you have copied a US template. UK recruiters notice immediately. Use British English throughout.

Writing in the third person

"Jane is a results-driven marketer" reads as stiff and dated. Use first person with the pronouns dropped: "Results-driven marketer with eight years of B2B SaaS experience". This is the modern UK convention.

Typically no — UK CVs usually omit a photo. You can simply leave the optional profile picture field empty. Photos can expose employers to discrimination claims under the Equality Act 2010, and most ATS platforms strip them anyway.
