US employers expect a one-page, achievement-driven resume with no photo, no personal details beyond contact information, and strong, quantified bullet points under each role. The US market has the most developed resume conventions in the world, and deviating from them is the fastest way to get rejected.

US resume conventions are governed partly by law and partly by recruiter expectations. Title VII of the Civil Rights Act prohibits employment discrimination on race, colour, religion, sex, or national origin. To avoid discrimination claims, US recruiters expect resumes to omit photos, age, marital status, religion, and any other protected characteristic. Including these details can get your application automatically rejected.

ATS usage is more widespread in the US than in any other market. Fortune 500 companies, mid-size firms, and even small businesses use platforms like Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, iCIMS, Taleo, and BrassRing. Switch on ATS Mode for any application going through an online portal — the single-column layout with standard section headings will parse reliably across all of these platforms.

Quantification is the single biggest differentiator in US resumes. "Responsible for managing a team" describes a job spec; "Built and retained a team of 12 that delivered 23% year-on-year revenue growth" describes what you actually did. The second version is what gets you the interview. US recruiters expect to see numbers in every bullet point — percentages, dollar amounts, time saved, users acquired, deals closed.

Action verbs carry particular weight in US resume writing. "Spearheaded", "orchestrated", "engineered", "shipped", "negotiated", "streamlined" — strong verbs convey leadership and ownership. Avoid weak constructions like "was responsible for", "helped with", or "assisted in". The AI assist tool can help you find stronger verbs for your existing bullets.

What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area, and one quantified achievement. Avoid objective statements — US recruiters expect a summary that sells what you bring, not what you want.

Work experience with quantified achievements

Each role should include job title, employer, dates (month and year), location, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Every bullet should have a number — percentage, dollar amount, time saved, users acquired. Quantification is not optional in the US market.

Education in a single section

Degree, institution, and graduation year. Recent graduates (within three years) can add coursework, honours, or GPA (if 3.5+). Experienced hires should keep this section to a single line per degree. Drop high school entirely once you have a university degree.

Skills section grouped by category

Technical skills, soft skills, and languages. Mirror the terminology used in the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match. US postings often reference specific frameworks (Salesforce, AWS, Agile) — use those exact terms.

Certifications (if relevant to the role)

Professional certifications (PMP, CPA, CFA, AWS, Six Sigma) are highly valued in the US. List them with the issuing organisation and date. For technical roles, cloud platform and framework-specific certifications are particularly valuable.

Optional sections that add depth

Projects, publications, patents, or volunteer experience can strengthen your application. Enable only what is relevant to the role. US recruiters appreciate brevity — every section should earn its place on the page.

Tips for a stronger resume

Keep it to one page if you have under ten years of experience

Two pages is acceptable for senior hires with extensive history. Three pages is acceptable only for academic, medical, or federal government applications. US recruiters will not read past the first page for most corporate roles.

Quantify every bullet point

Every bullet should have a number — percentage, dollar amount, time saved, users acquired, deals closed. "Led a team of 12 that delivered 23% year-on-year revenue growth" beats "Responsible for managing a team" every time.

Start every bullet with a strong action verb

"Spearheaded", "orchestrated", "engineered", "shipped", "negotiated", "streamlined". Avoid weak constructions like "was responsible for", "helped with", or "assisted in". The AI assist tool can help you find stronger verbs for your existing bullets.

Switch on ATS Mode for online portals

ATS usage is more widespread in the US than in any other market. Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, iCIMS, Taleo, and BrassRing are all commonly used. The single-column layout with standard section headings will parse reliably across all of these platforms.

Mirror the language of the job description

US postings often reference specific frameworks (Salesforce, AWS, Agile, Scrum) — use those exact terms. ATS systems match on terminology, and human recruiters scan for familiar terms. Just ensure the usage is natural — keyword stuffing is detected and penalised.

Save as PDF unless asked otherwise

PDF preserves your formatting on every device and operating system. DOCX files can render differently depending on the recipient's version of Word. Only send a DOCX if the employer explicitly requests an editable file.

Use US English spelling and conventions

"Organization" not "organisation", "analyze" not "analyse", "program" not "programme". Mixing British and American spelling signals carelessness. Mirror the language of the job posting if you are unsure which convention to use.

Common mistakes to avoid

Including a photo on a US resume

Photos are not included on US resumes. Title VII of the Civil Rights Act prohibits employment discrimination, and including a photo puts employers in a difficult position. Most ATS platforms strip images anyway, which can corrupt your layout. Save the headshot for your LinkedIn profile.

Listing personal details like age, marital status, or religion

These have no place on a US resume. They expose employers to discrimination claims under Title VII and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact details (name, email, phone, city, state) and professional information.

Going over one page without cause

Two pages is acceptable for senior hires. Three pages is acceptable only for academic, medical, or federal government applications. For most corporate roles, US recruiters will not read past the first page. Be ruthless about cutting outdated roles and irrelevant detail.

Using weak, passive language

"Was responsible for", "helped with", "assisted in" — these constructions signal a lack of ownership. Start every bullet with a strong action verb: "Spearheaded", "orchestrated", "engineered". Quantify outcomes wherever possible.

Including an objective statement

"Seeking a challenging role in a dynamic organisation" tells the recruiter nothing. Modern US resumes use a professional summary that positions you for the role, not an objective statement about what you want. The shift happened a decade ago — catch up.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do US resumes include a photo?

No — US resumes typically do not include a photo. Title VII of the Civil Rights Act prohibits employment discrimination, and including a photo puts employers in a difficult position. Leave the optional photo field empty.

How long should a US resume be?

Usually one page, especially for early-to-mid career applicants. Two pages is acceptable for senior hires with extensive history. Three pages is acceptable only for academic, medical, or federal government applications.

Should I include my GPA?

Include your GPA if it is 3.5 or above (out of 4.0). Below that, leave it off. US recruiters will assume the worst if the number is missing, but they will know the worst if it is there. Drop GPA entirely once you have three or more years of professional experience.

Can I include references on my resume?

US convention is to omit references from the resume itself. Use the line "References available on request" or omit the section entirely. Recruiters will ask for references later in the process, typically after the first interview.

Should I include my social security number?

No — never include your SSN on a resume. It is not needed at the application stage and including it puts you at risk of identity theft. Employers will collect it separately after a job offer is made.
