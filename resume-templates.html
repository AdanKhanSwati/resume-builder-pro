What to include

A targeted professional summary

Three to four lines that position you for the role. Mention your years of experience, your strongest skill area, and one quantified achievement. Update this for every application — a generic summary is a wasted opportunity.

Work experience in reverse chronological order

Most recent role first. Each entry should include title, employer, dates, and three to five achievement-focused bullets. Quantify outcomes wherever possible — numbers jump off the page and make your application memorable.

Education tailored to your career stage

Recent graduates: include coursework, honours, and capstone projects. Experienced hires: list degree, institution, and year only. Doctoral holders: include dissertation title and advisor if relevant to the role.

Skills grouped by category

Technical skills, soft skills, and languages. Mirror the terminology of the job description so both recruiters and ATS systems recognise the match. Five well-chosen skills beat twenty generic ones.

Optional sections that add depth

Projects, certifications, languages, volunteer experience, and achievements each have a dedicated module. Enable only the ones that strengthen your application for the specific role you are targeting.

Consistent formatting throughout

Pick one date format, one bullet style, and one font. Be consistent across every section. Inconsistencies signal carelessness to hiring managers. The template handles this automatically — you just fill in the content.

Tips for a stronger resume

Choose a template that matches your industry

Conservative industries (finance, law, government) prefer traditional layouts. Creative industries (design, marketing, media) tolerate more personality. Tech industries want clean, scannable documents. Match the template to the industry — the same content can succeed or fail based on the presentation.

Tailor the template for each application

Build your master version with everything you have ever done. For each application, duplicate the file and trim to the most relevant 80%. Adjust the summary, swap the order of your strengths, and mirror the language of the job posting. Five tailored versions beat fifty generic ones.

Switch on ATS Mode for online applications

When applying through Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, or iCIMS, switch on ATS Mode. The simplified single-column layout parses more reliably and reduces the chance of your information being jumbled or dropped.

Save as PDF unless asked otherwise

PDF preserves your formatting on every device and operating system. DOCX files can render differently depending on the recipient's version of Word. Only send a DOCX if the employer explicitly requests an editable file.

Run a final consistency check

Check that every date format matches, every bullet ends the same way, and every employer name is spelled out consistently. The template handles most of this automatically, but a final manual check catches anything the editor missed.

Test the PDF on a phone screen

Many recruiters first review applications on mobile. Open your exported PDF on your phone and check that the text is readable, the layout holds together, and there are no awkward breaks across pages. The live preview in this template shows you exactly what recruiters will see.

Save your data as JSON for backup

Use the Save JSON button regularly. If your browser storage is cleared, you can re-import your data and pick up where you left off. The JSON file is small enough to email to yourself or store in any cloud drive.

Common mistakes to avoid

Choosing style over ATS compatibility

A two-column template with a sidebar looks beautiful, but many ATS platforms cannot parse it. For online applications, always use ATS Mode. Save the styled version for direct emails and networking events.

Using a template without customisation

Templates are a starting point, not a finished document. Every section should be tailored to your specific story and the role you are applying for. A copy-paste template with placeholder text still visible is worse than no template at all.

Including irrelevant personal details

Age, marital status, religion, and national ID numbers have no place on a modern resume in most countries. They expose employers to discrimination claims and add nothing to your application. Stick to contact information and professional details.

Picking a template that does not match your industry

A heavily designed infographic template sends the wrong signal in conservative industries. A plain, traditional template undersells you in creative industries. Match the template to the industry — recruiters notice the disconnect.

Going over two pages without cause

Senior hires with 15+ years of experience can justify a two-page resume. Anyone else should aim for one. If you are struggling to fit, cut the oldest roles, drop the interests section, and tighten your bullet points. Three pages is too many for most corporate roles.
