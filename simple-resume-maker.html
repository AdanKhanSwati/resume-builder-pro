What to include

Contact details at the top

Full name, professional email, phone number, and city. Add LinkedIn and portfolio URLs only if they are polished and current. Skip the full street address — city and country are enough for most applications.

A short summary or headline

Two lines that describe who you are professionally. "Marketing manager with eight years of B2B SaaS experience" is more useful than a long narrative paragraph. Recruiters scan this first to decide whether to keep reading.

Work experience, most recent first

Job title, employer, dates, and three to five bullet points per role. Focus on outcomes — what changed because you were there — rather than a list of responsibilities copied from the job description.

Education in a single section

Degree, institution, and graduation year. If you are a recent graduate, add relevant coursework and honours. Experienced hires can keep this to one line per degree.

A skills section grouped by category

Technical skills, soft skills, and languages, each as a short list. Mirror the terminology from the job description so both recruiters and ATS software recognise the match.

Optional: certifications and projects

If you have professional certifications (CPA, PMP, AWS) or notable projects, add a section for each. Keep these focused and recent — anything older than five years can be dropped unless particularly prestigious.

Tips for a stronger resume

Keep the design invisible

The best simple document is one where the recruiter remembers the content, not the layout. Avoid colours, graphics, and decorative fonts. Black text on white paper, with one accent colour for headings at most, is the sweet spot.

Use one font throughout

Pick Arial, Calibri, or Helvetica in 10-12pt and stick with it. Mixing fonts is the fastest way to make a simple document look amateurish. Bold for headings, regular for body — that is the entire typographic system you need.

Be consistent with bullet points

Choose one bullet style (round, square, or dash) and use it everywhere. End every bullet with a period, or none of them — pick one convention and stick with it. Consistency signals attention to detail.

Limit yourself to one page if possible

If you have under ten years of experience, one page is enough. Two pages is acceptable for senior hires. Three pages is too many unless you are in academia or research and have a list of publications to include.

Save as PDF for universal compatibility

PDF preserves your formatting on every device and operating system. DOCX files can render differently depending on the recipient's version of Word. Choose PDF unless an employer explicitly asks for an editable file.

Read it out loud before sending

Reading aloud catches typos, awkward phrasing, and run-on sentences that the eye skips over. It takes three minutes and saves you from sending a document with an embarrassing mistake.

Test it on a phone screen

Many recruiters first review applications on mobile. If your document is unreadable on a 6-inch screen — text too small, layout broken, headings lost — you have lost the opportunity before it began.

Common mistakes to avoid

Adding graphics, icons, or progress bars

Visual flourishes like skill bars ("Photoshop: 80%") and infographic-style icons look modern but confuse ATS software and date quickly. Stick to plain text — it is universal, parses reliably, and never goes out of style.

Using a tiny font to fit more content

Going below 10pt to squeeze in extra bullet points backfires — recruiters will not read text they have to squint at. Cut content instead. Tight editing beats dense packing every time.

Including personal photos in markets that discourage them

US, UK, and Canadian applications should not include a photo. They expose employers to bias claims and many ATS platforms strip images anyway. Reserve the headshot for your LinkedIn profile.

Listing hobbies that add no professional value

"Reading, music, and travel" tells the recruiter nothing useful. Drop the interests section entirely unless you have a hobby that directly reinforces your professional story (open-source contributions for a developer, marathon running for a sales role, etc.).

Sending the document as a link to a Google Doc

Recruiters will not click unknown links. Always attach a PDF (or DOCX if requested) directly to the email or application portal. A link looks like you could not be bothered to export the file properly.
